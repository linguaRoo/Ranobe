Meta-X: Origins

“Genesis of Fire”

Blond hair was the first thing you would notice about him. It was probably the last thing to notice about him, too. He wasn’t the most notable face in any crowd, especially not his class photo. Especially with the two people who’d been placed next to him. Well, maybe people would notice the one person between the two most noticeable of his classmates. “Yeah, and maybe Hell will freeze over,” Jason said, brushing back his hair.

Lately Jason’s hair had taken to standing up, and he was definitely more noticeable than the young man he was talking to, but he was glad to have such a reliable best friend. “Nate, you need to stop obsessing over this,” he said, pulling the yearbook out of his friend’s hands. “People aren’t going to look back on this yearbook and say, ‘Look at that loser between Kathy and Jim,’ man.” Jason shoved the yearbook back in his friend’s backpack, and looked around.

“I know that, Jase,” Nathaniel said, scratching his left arm. “But I’m practically a nobody. Nobody pays attention, nobody talks to me,”

“Don’t say that,” Jason said, slapping his friend. “You think that way you are a nobody. If you don’t, you might get to be a somebody, someday. Besides, the popular girls are always asking you for help on their homework.”

“Not my fault they’re so lazy,” Nathaniel said, chuckling. Jason was right, though. School photos always left him depressed, and obsessing wasn’t going to help. “Hey, did you know that Alice’s birthday is coming up?”

“Hard not to know,” Jason said, grinning. “But she didn’t invite me to the party.”

Nathaniel’s jaw dropped. “Why would she invite me and not you?” She wasn’t the most popular girl in school, but she was definitely inviting them. And the more popular guys too. It was a very confusing thought, that he would get invited without his best friend.

“Maybe she likes you,” Jason said, tossing his bookbag towards the school building. “We can think about it later, bro. Time for our practice.”

“Yeah, mom needs me home earlier than usual today,” Nathaniel said, dropping his backpack. “So let’s go!”

A flurry of punches fron Jason ended the fight, as Nathaniel looked at his watch. “Shoot. Two minutes to cover ten minutes of ground,” he said, heading over to his backpack. “Have a good day, I’ll see you in class tomorrow. Mom is gonna be so ticked.”

He ran home, and checked the visible parts of his body for bruises. As long as he was safe there, he wouldn’t be in any trouble. Except for the being late part. Pausing for a moment outside, he caught his breath and brushed his hair out of his face. He went inside, expecting to be yelled at.

His mother’s response shocked him.

“You know your birthday is coming up,” she said, looking at him. Less angry than he’d expected. “Don’t be late again or you’ll lose presents. Do you understand me?”

“Yes, mom,” he said, taking off his backpack. “Why do you need me home early, anyway?”

“We need to talk about your plans for middle school,” she said, motioning towards the room behind him. “You’re the smartest student in your class, Nathaniel, and we think you should go to the best middle school.”

“She’s right,” his dad said, as Nathaniel entered the living room. He was losing his hair, which he attributed to raising four sons, but was otherwise in fairly good shape for a man in his late thirties. “I think your mother is jumping the gun a bit, but if you wait too long, City’s positions for incoming students may be filled.”

“City? Maybe for high school, but I don’t want to leave my friends just for the sake of middle school,” Nathaniel said. “And they don’t have to make any plans yet. Besides, there’s still more than a whole year before I need to decide.”

“All we’re saying is to think about it,” his mother said. “And make sure your grades don’t start dropping, understand?”

“Yeah,” he said. He rolled his eyes as he turned around. “And no computer games before I finish my homework.” He left the room angry, and went up to his bedroom. He started on his homework.

“Moving?” Alice looked up at her parents in shock. “We’re moving at the end of the school year?” Alice had an Asian look to her, which made sense since both of her parents were Chinese. Her black hair reached to the middle of her back, and her brown eyes almost seemed red in her anger. “Isn’t there any other choice?”

“Only if you want to live in poverty,” Chan said, looking straight at her. Her father was one of the few people who could meet her angry gaze without flinching. “My job position is moving, daughter. This is our only way. Please don’t be angry with me or your mother.”

“He’ s right,” Meilin said, placing her hand on her daughter’s shoulder. “We’ll have plenty of time to make sure your friends know, Alice. We promise it will be all right.”

“How can you say that,” Alice said, fighting back her tears. “This is the best place I’ve ever lived, and I don’t want to leave. Why can’t you just find another job, papa?”
