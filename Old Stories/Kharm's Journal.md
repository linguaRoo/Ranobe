# Late Journey - The Journal of Hunter “Kharm” Carter

## 4 April 2001

It has been so long getting to this point, but I am finally on the road. Due to the weak League presence in the Midwest United States, and the fact that my parents forced me to stay through high school before allowing me to try training monsters, we had agreed that I would go to Japan on my training voyage. Japan was the best area of the world to be a trainer at the turn of the 21st century, with more than a dozen organized conferences under the banner of the Japanese National Monster Training League, itself a division of the World Monster Federation.

Japan is where I was come the start of April 2001. My things were all set. I had studied Japanese throughout junior high and high school, and after a nice long flight by jet then helicopter, I was about to start my journey from the place known as Sapling Town, or Naegimura in Japanese. As the helicopter came in, I could see that it was a very small village. Near the center were a handful of small apartment blocks, with a lake to the east and some modern farms to the north, but the biggest thing in town was the gleaming white and glass of the Utsugi Research Center, with its attached ranch-lands.

The helicopter set down near the western edge of town, and the pilot hustled me off with my gear. “Good journey,” he said in heavily accented English, and then he headed off into town.

---

Sapling Town was the place where my friend Tenpo, who I only knew online, had grown up and started his journey. It had been six years, and Tenpo had e-mailed me to congratulate me on finally graduating.

I told him I was visiting his hometown to start my journey. He’d pushed me into accepting his mother’s hospitality for the night. I was about a mile from the research center, and maybe three miles from the city center. Maps always seemed to make things look so small.

As I walked into the town limits, I could tell very plainly that Sapling Town was a rural place. Still, it was home to one of the world’s foremost researchers of monster powers, Dr. Utsugi. The helicopter faded into the distance behind me as I walked into town, and there was no turning back now. I had, after all, always wanted to be a trainer. A little walk now was nothing.

After about fifteen minutes, I approached the research center and got a much clearer view of it than the helicopter had afforded me. The facility was a lot like a farm. The main building was similar to a ranch-style home with two floors – a ground floor with an extension in the west that was surely the research center, a second floor that probably was little more than sleeping quarters for the doctor, his family, and his associates, and a fenced-in patio above that, clearly a place where one could watch over the town or the research farm. The whole facility was fenced in and green. The extension seemed to be where I would go to meet the doctor, and it was a modern place with tinted glass windows instead of sturdy wooden walls. I approached the door, which slid open as I got near, and walked inside.

Inside was a miracle of modern technology. A shining research lab where I could see a dozen scientists observing various monsters. There were a lot of things marking me as an outsider here. My height, at over six feet tall, and my light brown hair were starters, but a more immediate mark of being an outsider was my body shape.

I am a furre. A human-born and human-shaped canine. I personally look like a humanoid dog, with physical traits that are a mix of golden retriever, german shepherd, and wolf. My ears are pointed, my shape is big and powerful, and my fur is gold. My eyes are a stormy grey, though they are ostensibly blue. A lot of furres have flat, human-like feet, but mine are naturally raised, padded, and otherwise entirely wolf-like. As a result of this, I have spent most of my life barefoot, as there are no shoes are made to fit my feet.

“Ah, Carter-san!” The man approaching me was a man with hair colored like my own, though he was clearly in his late 30s or perhaps early 40s, and his hair was much thinner than mine. He looked me over attentively, seeming to take in the details of my furred physiology, and then spoke up again. “You must be Mr. Carter, correct? From America?”

“Uh, yes,” I said, smiling. “And you must be Doctor Utsugi. It’s good to meet you.”

“Yes. I’m afraid I haven’t got your things all ready to go yet.” He seemed a bit flustered and distracted. “Should I send one of my assistants with you to help you with your things? Will you need help finding the hotel?”

“No, thank you.” I bowed politely, still not used to doing that with my travel pack on my back. “My friend, Tenpo Hikari, grew up here. His mother has agreed to let me stay in his room tonight.”

“I see, I see.” He pulled a pair of glasses out of his lab coat and smiled. “I have your guide and trainer’s license, and a starting kit, but I’m afraid your partner is being stubborn today. We’ve looked all over and can’t find him anywhere.”

I had gone through a selection process with Dr. Utsugi over the past month, choosing my starting monster partner carefully from the selection he had at his facility. He had first shown me thirty specimens, a variety of male and females, mostly of three standard varieties of monsters.

In the end, though, I had chosen to take a different route than the standard. Most trainers took a rare species of one of the common elements – grass, water, or fire – that were difficult to find outside of League-approved facilities. I had chosen to go with a mundane species, one we called ratty in English, and which the Japanese league officially names chiratta. As a note, I will be sticking with English names as I write this, for convenience’ sake alone.

“Yes. This little ratty seems to be somewhere in the…” he paused as I heard a skittering sound in the floor. “Kengo, she’s in the ducts up here. Moving toward the house!”

One of the research aides ran up and followed the sound of the monster in the air ducts. “I see. Perhaps she is nervous about meeting me. I’ll let you work on this, and return in the morning.”

“Of course,” the doctor said, tapping his foot impatiently on the floor. “She’s a spirited one. Precocious, too. Ah, yes. Goodbye, Mr. Carter.”

“Yes. Until tomorrow,” I answered, bowing before I headed out.

---

I wandered around the town for a while before I finally decided to visit Tenpo’s mother. He lived in a small home, a two-story apartment in a row of apartments just like it. A black haired woman with her hair in a bun was working in the garden as I approached. “Mrs. Hikari?”

“Yes?” She looked up and smiled at me. “Are Mr. Carter? I thought you would be shorter.”

I smiled and looked over her work. She was growing tulips and weeding her garden for the new year. “I often hear that I’m very tall. By the way, my mother also grows tulips.”

She smiled and stood up. “I have so much time with Alric on his travels and Tenpo gone on his journey. He’s gone up north, now, did you know?”

I did know that. I had also seen Alric years ago on a television program. He studied the so-called “legendary” monsters, like the various types of phoenices, the ones called forces of nature, and the ones who some called gods. Tenpo had met him about a year into his journey, and helped him on a few of his research quests.

“He mentioned it, ma’am.” I paused, not sure the proper way to ask, politely, about going inside. After about 30 seconds, my body gave me a good excuse. “I’m sorry, but I need to use a toilet.”

“Ah, I’m sorry. You must want to get that heavy backpack off, too,” she said, covering her mouth. “Please, come inside. I’ll show you where you can set your things.”

The inside was a nice home. It had a nice wide window in the back of the kitchenette are, right over the sink, back and to the left. The main room was divided by furniture into a dining space, just in from of the kitchenette, a television space, in front of that, and a small library with complicated-looking books–sure, I could read Japanese, but books like these would take a bit more effort than I cared to admit.

My friend Jason had come here after he turned ten. Actually, that wasn’t quite true. He’d gone to Slate Town, east of here, and traveled with a couple of guys who started the same time he did–one called Wolf and the other a local named Hiroshi. I hadn’t heard much from him in the past five years, but he kept in touch enough that I knew he was alright and enjoying his journey and everything he was doing on it. Jason had been my best friend back in grade school, and I hoped I would run into him, even though he said his latest work was a secret, and he couldn’t tell me about it.

I blinked a few times, shaking my head as I heard Tenpo’s mother calling my name. “Sorry, sorry. I just remembered a friend who started his journey years ago.”

“I see. There are two bedrooms and a toilet upstairs. Tenpo’s room is the closest door to the stairs. You can set your things in there then use the toilet, and I’ll start making dinner.”

I nodded and bowed politely, hurrying up the stairs and slipping my backpack off outside of the first door I saw. The toilet was a welcome relief, and I sighed as I left the room after washing my hands. I went back to my backpack and opened the door marked “Tenpo’s Room”.

The room was kept clean, but it was clear Tenpo hadn’t lived here for years. The bedroom was a fair room, about 12 feet by 10 feet, with his bed in the far corner. I set my bag down and looked at his collection of figurines, near the window, and his certificate of achievement from the Western Conference – he had been the fourth-place contestant in the League Conference his first season, and had chosen not to participate since. He focused on the adventure of finding new varieties of monsters instead of proving his prowess as a battler.

I wasn’t sure what my plan was. I would definitely visit a few of the League’s gym locations. A Gym, in league terms, serves two purposes – it’s first, and foremost, a place where like-minded monster trainers can get together and share techniques and strategies for raising Pokemon, and even test and build their own skills. But in regards to conferences, a League Gym was also a place where a League-certified representative could grant a badge of honor – more or less a special stamp in a passport-like book. With eight such badges, you qualified to enter a league conference, where you would pit your monsters and training skills against the best in the region.

I had wanted, when I was ten, to come with Jason and participate in the Eastern Conference. My parents had said, “No journey until you graduate from high-school.” So I had gone through high school, read the great contemporary researchers like Yukinari Oozawa, Utsugi, and Alric Hikari. Not that Hikari published much, but his writings were still important for understand the mythological aspect of monsters. I’ll summarize some of it for anybody who isn’t well read in his work.

---

_Before recorded history, humans, animals, and monsters were the same. Many questions are asked about this, but research has shown that pre-city humans did live in a naturally balanced relationship with the land and its creatures. Before cities, humans and monsters in a region ate and lived and traveled together, working, hunting, etc. According to some old myths, they had even loved each other without regard for species. According to one of Alric’s theories, this may be where certain naturally anthropomorphic monster-like species came from._

_What is known for certain is that around 30,000 years ago, we have evidence of monsters and humans living together, with no evidence of capture or control devices. By about 20,000 years ago, only a fraction of monster species were still living with humans. By the earliest cities, around 10,000 years ago, only a few of the most docile species were still living with us. Around 5,000 years ago, the earliest capture devices were made in the Middle East using magic (which explain magic elsewhere, I promise.)_

_At their peak, the Romans could control three monsters with their most advanced capture device, which had to be worn on the back and which only a soldier or gladiator was strong enough to handle for long. This ability was lost in the dark ages, when mystics overwhelmed technologists, and during this time, eastern mystics in China and Japan invented a technique called binding – a method of containing a monster in an object when it was not battling. During this time, a few conflicts occurred between monsters and humans, and the anthropomorphic races hid themselves away._

_By the renaissance, this knowledge was beginning to return to Europe, but they had driven many of the monsters out due to fear of devilry and witchcraft in association with them. The minds of the enlightenment who studied them did so in constant fear of persecution from the Catholic church. Some were burned at the stake for witchcraft._

_It was no surprise, then, that the American colonists were not monster users. The practice was eventually reconsidered, but they used monsters in the same manner as normal animals – no capture devices, not control methods, just working with domesticated creatures. After the second World War, research focused primarily on electronics and computers and on monsters. Genetic research showed that we humans are related to monsters. In the past 50 years, we developed a way to blend the magic and science to create capture balls – portable devices that can hold a monster outside of battle. With the right materials, higher effectiveness in capture can be achieved, and rumor has it that about 10 years ago, the Japanese Sylph Corporation was able to make a capture ball so effective it couldn’t fail._

_But that’s history to talk about elsewhere. I was supposed to be writing about Doctor Hikari’s theory. Doctor Hikari believes that furres are a side-effect of our shared ancestry with animals and monsters, that sometimes certain traits normally unexpressed in humans express themselves to produce a furre. Furres are genetically human – no known genetic test can yet predict at birth if a normal-looking human infant is a furre. It is known that the children of two furre parents are more likely than average to be a furre, though through most of history, being a furre was about as common as being left-handed (though furres are no more likely to be left-handed than a normal human)._

_So he studies the place of legendary monsters in our history and our world today, and his associate, Doctor Emmett Drake, focuses on the genetics. Alric proposes from his research that legends come in sets of three – often linked by some theme. He cites the thunder-bird, the phoenix, and the frost-bird, said in some places to appear to people who were on the verge of dying of exposure to the cold in harsh mountains. He goes on to say that there is some support that these trios are often guarded or ruled by some other monster. In this example, he cites the monsters believed to represent the aurora, lightning strikes, and volcanic heat, and how they are believed to have been imbued with their power by a divine phoenix-king._

Of course, this is old hat. Tenpo wrote me in 1995 to say that he witnessed the rise of the leviathan known in southern japan as the ocean-god, and its behemoth counterpart known as the heart of the volcano. In 1996, he ventured to inland China and witnessed the death and rebirth of a lesser phoenix (he sent me a video of it). He’s seen legendary things that support his father’s theories.

That’s what Alric Hikari has made his life’s work. Studying legends and myths and powerful creatures that seem like gods to us.

---

After I had settled in, and checked my e-mail with Tenpo’s old PC, I headed downstairs. The afternoon and evening were fairly uneventful. I ate dinner with Tenpo’s mom, she made spaghetti with garlic toast. After dinner we just talked about Tenpo for a while, until I decided to go to bed. After all, tomorrow was going to be a big day, as I started my journey.

---

I’d never been an early riser, but today was a special day, so I’d set my alarm early so I could be showered and ready to go to Dr. Utsugi’s as soon as possible. Tenpo’s mom prepared a good American-style breakfast for me—bacon and eggs, with toast and a freshly sliced apple– which was going to be my last homemade meal for quite a while.

After breakfast I thanked her for her hospitality, and then headed out to Dr. Utsugi’s laboratory. Sapling Town was fairly quiet place this early in the morning, so I had no trouble making my way there without running in any strangers. The doctor was already waiting for me at the door.

“Good morning, doctor,” I said, putting my best smile on. I’ve been looking forward to this day for eight years I wasn’t going to look upset, even if I did hate early mornings.

“Good morning Kharm.” He seems to be in a very good to know. “We were able to get your monster all squared up last night. She’s waiting in the lab to meet you, along with the other gear you’re going to need on your journey.”

“Thank you very much, doctor,” I replied, smiling warmly. That at least, was actually good news. I had brought all of the traveling equipment I would meet, however I was relying on the doctor to provide all the gear that the League was going to require me to have. I followed him into the laboratory, and into the private lab where he was giving me my monster.

The room was a small, sterile room. It had a small lab counter, on which were sitting a trainer ID, a bundle of salves and capture balls, a portable monster guide, and lastly the small purple-furred rat that was to be my monster partner. As the doctor had pointed out, this monster was a female of her species, and so her whiskers were a bit shorter than was typically seen, as most trainers captured male specimens. She was napping in a small cage next to which was a special capture ball, which I recognized to be the style of ball used for all starting monsters.

“I’ve already filled out your trainer ID,” said the doctor. “However, it’s up to you to actually capture your ratty. This is just the way we do things here in the Japanese League, I know things might be different in America.”

I nodded, and picked up starter ball. Utsugi opened the cage, and I put my hand in near the sleeping ratty. As the trigger button on the starter ball touched the ratty’s nose I felt a small click, as the ball opened slightly and ratty transformed into energy which was sucked inside of the ball. This is how capture balls work, by turning the monster into an energy form which is able to live inside of the ball. In addition to this, starter balls are specially made so that only specially domesticated monsters can be placed inside them, these monsters know what is happening and don’t fight the process. After a ball is used to capture a monster that ball and that monster are bound together, the monster can be recalled to the ball easily, and the ball cannot capture another monster.

Now that I had captured ratty, I was ready to begin my journey. For several years I had already had an idea of what I was going to name my starter. After settling on a ratty with Dr. Utsugi, I’d had to change my plans. However, at this point, I’d settled on the name Stacia. The doctor asked if I wanted to meet my monster here or if I wanted to wait until I left the lab. I had already decided, before I left Tenpo’s mom, to meet Stacia here and ask her how she liked the name I had chosen for her.

---

_Monsters are intelligent, presumably even sapient. All the scientific research has shown this, as the majority of monsters form some type of community of their own kind, even in the wild. These communities often show the basic signs of civilization in the same ways that the remains of early human civilizations do. Some even seem to use some form of currency, often the peculiar berries that are grown nearest to their homes. By the same token, monsters are able to communicate, though not in plain English or any other human language. Most often they do this through primitive vocalizations, some of which resemble human speech. It is worth noting that those monsters which have vocalizations of this type have the most consistent names throughout human cultures_.

---

I released my Ratty and she seemed docile, for now. “Hello there, little one. My name is Kharm.” She looked up at me, and I knelt down and held my hand out slowly. My wolf-like form might have made her less skittish than she would be towards a normal human. “And I’d like to call you Stacia. Do you like that name?”

From the corner of my eyes, I could see Utsugi smiling and nodding. Ratty reached her forepaws up towards my hand and put them on my outstretched fingers. “Well, Stacia,” I said softly, “I think we’re going to be friends.”
