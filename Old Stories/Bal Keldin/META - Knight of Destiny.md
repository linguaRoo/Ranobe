# Knight of Destiny: Bal Keldin

The second iteration of Bal debuted three years after the finale of the original Bal Keldin. It reimagined Bal as a demigod, a child of the Gramurn god Khyarm, who needed to achieve glory to ascend to the heavens and godhood.

The series was published using a folio model, though it was produced and published on a 4-week cycle rather than a 6-week cycle. This gave it 18 folio volumes per year. Its initial run lasted for 6 years, with a 3 year expansion following after a two year hiatus. For another 18 years, the author continued to produce special issues and illustrated novels.

His favorite part of the project was working with experts to develop monster, spirit, and deity designs for the series. He often contributed original creature designs to other _méten_ stories after the initial run completed.

There was often a drastic difference between the art style on the cover (which was highly rendered and showed excessive muscular details) and the internal style, which was simplified for the sake of producing around 30 pages of story per month. 

## Synopsis

### Introduction

The son of a warrior woman who has never known his father is coming of age. His possesses greater strength than the rest of his litter, and often feels out place. After coming of age and making his first kill in a hunt, he lays his offering to the tribe upon the pyre, and announces his adult name: Balan Kiltaä, though he is often referred to within the story as Bal in order to hide from the “great sorrows.”

From the fire of the altar, an Ōraiganon emerges and hisses out his name. This spirit of stone and fire attacks Bal, providing no reason for the attack. Because his tribe takes this attack as a bad omen, Bal must outwit and defeat the spiritual beast before her can learn the truth. His mother reveals that the Ōreiganon has been sealed into the altar by Khyarm, several years before Bal’s birth, and that Khyarm, the god of warriors, was his father.

Warned that other spirits would seek to quiet him now that he was an adult, he was advised by the shaman Rulukh to travel to the great Shrine of the Gods in the east. He was to be accompanied by his brother Garon Rausi, and was joined in his travels by a warrior woman named Winaän Liyara. Communing with Khyarm at the shrine, Bal’s origin was confirmed. His father said that there was no way to stop the great sorrows from pursuing him, and his only option was to be trained by the great trainer of heroes, Sharn Hwau.

### Main Storyline

Bal chose Liyara (sometimes styled as Liyer) as his companion, stating that a brother might make him reckless, but a sister would give him reason to survive every battle. Garon pledged to become a howlstirrer for Bal, the one who would begin spreading the glories of his heroic brother, and they parted ways for a time.

Sharn Hwau took Bal and Liyer in, training them for three years before a Gaurin, a massive creature like an ape with the wings and roar of a dragon, finally attacked them at his home. While he was injured, he warned Bal not to become consumed by a desire for vengeance. Before sending Bal out to earn glory, he reminded Bal of the most important lesson of Knighthood: The mark of a Knight is mercy for their foes, after achieving victory.

### Finale

During the final year of the original 6-year run, Bal overcame a great plot by Rramagorm (the god of the underworld) to use an army of great sorrows to imprison Naigumi (the great mother of the gods). Bal was offered his place among the Gods of the Gramurn, finally meeting his father Khyarm face to face, but refused to ascend, choosing to live the rest of his mortal life with Liyara. The epilogue was a text story included in Folio 108 in which Garon tells the story of their death and post-mortem ascension, together, to the heavens as the God of Destiny and Goddess of Devotion, and into the stars as the constellation known as “the lovers.”

The epilogue makes it clear that these stories have not told every legend of the Knight of Destiny, but that this was the howling that earned him a place among the Gods.

### Continuations

#### The Titanic War Against Heaven

The first expansion of the story begins when Balan and Liyara are raising a litter with Liyara’s family and their peaceful days are broken by the sound of a dragon—or a Gaurin. Taking up their arms together, the two vow to protect their pack. They find that the seal Bal had placed upon the Gaurin who had once attacked their master (Sharn Hwau) has broken, but that the Gaurin called them as a warning: Rramagorm, forbidden from the surface and heavens after his plot, has dug deep into the earth and found monsters older than any the Gramurn have seen before.

This series was produced on a 6-week, or standard (Atrusian) monthly schedule—allowing more detailed interior art than the original run—with a series of arcs against each of 6 “elemental” ancestors, gigantic monsters who had been lying dormant deep within the underworld. The first monster released was known as the Mother of the Aurelg and Gaurin, a giant beast of elemental earth whose every step shook all of the land and crushed trees like grass underfoot. The other great ancestral monsters were great giants of boiling water, molten earth, fungal consumption, northern ice, and wasting disease—which had overtaken Rramagorm himself.

Because he held to the lesson of mercy, Bal did not slay these monsters, but returned them to the deep prisons where they had once lay, and even saved his old foe from the wasting disease that consumed him, and Bal even restored Rramagorm’s place in the heavens. For her own part in these deeds, Liyara was promised by the gods that she would not die before her eternal mate, and would ascend to the heavens with him.

#### Other Continuations

The periodic stories told after this were smaller in scope, focusing on local heroics or Bal becoming entangled in inter-species conflicts and fights against great monsters from other cultures across Atrusius.

## Notes for Gaean Readers

The Gramurn are a species of anthropomorphic wolfkin, and their senses differ in range and precision than those of normal humans. While the most prominent of these senses (thermal perception) is seldom depicted in _Knight of Destiny_, the author consulted with many Gramurn advisors on how to properly depict their range of abilities.

Gramurn and other life forms native to Atrusius typically have only four digits per limb.
