Melanie sighed as she sat on the provided, and uncomfortable, bed. It had taken almost two hours for her dad to set up the computer, but the room was finally her own. This was that start of her life, she told herself, looking over the cramped bedroom in her dorm. There were two separate bedrooms in the dorm room, a single bathroom for the two of them, and they shared a shower with the next dorm over. Kelsie, the RA, had told her that she shouldn’t leave anything in the shower she didn’t want other people using.

The main room was an all-in-one room with a fridge and microwave, plus some cupboards with dry erase nameplates on them. Sarah had already written her name on the cupboard furthest from the fridge, and Melanie was still deciding if she wanted the one next to the fridge, or the one in the middle. They both had their benefits, after all.

She decided that it would be best to make her bed as comfortable as possible before she did too much. The school provided basic furnishings for the dorm, but they were sturdy, durable, and barely comfortable at best. They were also heavy, so re-arranging the room was not really an option. As she opened her suitcase, she let herself get lost in thought.

She’d met Sarah’s brother during the move-in, but Chaz had seemed a bit preoccupied by something. It was a shame he hadn’t noticed her gazes, or maybe it was a good thing. It turned out that though he looked older than Sarah, he was her younger brother. Maybe it had just been the unshaved stubble, which Sarah didn’t seem to care for. What had she said?

“Be a dear and shave before I come home,” seemed like the right thing. He’d responded with a dull, “Okay,” before Sarah had given him a sisterly kiss on the cheek and he left. It seemed like they had a good relationship, and Melanie wondered what it was like to have siblings who actually cared about you.

She finally got her sheets out. They were black with random green text, like in that movie with the robots. She was careful with the comforter, since that was the part that had glow-in-the-dark pigment, but the sheets and blanket just had the text. She’d had to spend her own allowance to buy the set, since Dorothy rarely liked to spend money on things Melanie really wanted. Even for birthdays, Melanie usually had to beg for a vacation that everybody would enjoy, and she still got the short end of the stick on those.

“Being in the middle sucks.”

Melanie blinked and snapped her head to the door. “What do you mean?”

“You were mumbling about your birthdays,” Sarah said with a gentle smile. “Sorry, I just wanted to check in. I didn’t mean to eavesdrop, but it sounds like you were a middle child too.”

“Your brother seems to adore you,” Melanie retorted as she threw her fitted sheet over the mattress. “We can’t be that alike.”

“Well we won’t know if we don’t compare notes,” Sarah said, stepping into the room and looking around. “Wait, you got a real computer? You’re really serious about the programming thing, aren’t you?”

“Didn’t want to eavesdrop, but you don’t seem to mind snooping now,” Melanie grumbled. “The only good thing Dorothy ever really got me. I think it’s her way of saying ‘good riddance.’ If you’re in here anyway, would you mind helping me with this?”

“Sure, so… Neo or Trinity?” Sarah grabbed one corner and helped get it on the bed.

Melanie blushed as bit, but stared at the bed as she worked on the sheets. “I felt like Neo was too predictable and flat. Not that Trinity was much better.”

As Melanie got her bed made, they talked about the movie and other movies with stronger females. “Ripley was better in the first movie and the newer ones are just trying to grab for money.”

“Well, I’ve always had a soft spot for Sarah Connor,” Sarah said. “I mean, she’s training for a robot apocalypse and still manages to support those tits? Come on, the only reason those movies work is the sex appeals of Sarah and the T-800, right?”

Melanie let out a sudden heavy sigh. “I guess so. I wish we could get another Terminator movie, though.”

“Be careful what you wish for,” Sarah said with a chuckle. “Did you know I never got watch girl cartoons? I just had to let my big brothers control the TV. I finally watched some of them when we got cable a few years ago. Turned out they were just as bad as the ones my brothers watched.”

“I never got to pick anything at home,” Melanie sighed. “Once I turned 14, dad and Dorothy let me see movies on my own, but I always had to use the family computer and the living room TV and Cal and Martha’s hand-me-downs. I’m lucky I even know I want to study computers.”

“Is Dorothy your mom, then?” Sarah took a seat on the floor, her legs crossed, and look up to Melanie. “Or…”

“Step-mother,” Melanie almost spat it out. “Dad couldn’t raise the three of us alone. She was his second girlfriend after mom passed, maybe three years? Then she had Josiah. She treats Cal and Martha like they matter, and Josiah can do no wrong as long as he’s not actually touching me. So yeah… maybe you’re right. Being in the middle sucks.”

“Well, they said the student union is open from move-in day, right? You got a meal plan, or want me to get you something?”

“I… sure. That sounds nice, Sarah.”

They got lost twice, finding their way into the media center and library first, then somehow winding up in front of the Computer Science building before Melanie actually grabbed a copy of the campus map. “Come on, we’re here, and the student union is… wait, do you know which way is north?”

“Our dorm is in the south part of the campus,” Sarah said. “So we started over…” She pointed to a building and started tracing something like the path they’d walked. “And there’s the library. Then we made it here. So north is…” She turned and faced North Street. “And the student union is… back in the South Quad by our dorm?”

Melanie laughed at how red Sarah got when she realized how far off they had wandered. “Okay, but it’s been good company at least.”

“I’m not good company,” Sarah said with a frustrated groan. “If you think I am you’d better watch out or else I’ll turn you gay.”

“You can’t turn me gay,” Melanie said with a laugh. “That’s not a real thing.”

“That’s what… That sounds like something I heard before,” Sarah said, pausing in the middle of the sidewalk, trying to figure out why it sounded familiar. After a few seconds, she shook her head and sighed. “I can do anything I want, I’m the best.”

Melanie just laughed and followed the map back to the south quad, idly talking with Sarah about their study plans. Sarah wanted to be a psychologist and hoped to help people understand themselves better. Melanie wanted to get a job in Redmond working on big software projects, maybe even some systems work.

Finally, they made it back to the quad and stared at the entrance to their dorm building from the front of The Market, the student union cafeteria and store on their end of the campus.

“This was needlessly complicated,” Sarah blurted out. “And I have never gotten so lost, not even when I was camping in the wilderness!”

“I never got to go camping,” Melanie said. “Dorothy always made us go to resorts and theme parks for my birthday, since it was in July.”

“Whatever. Tonight’s dinner is on me,” Sarah said, looking over Melanie with a hungry glint in her eyes.

“Th… Thanks,” Melanie said, blushing as she wondered if Sarah had, maybe, been serious about what she’d said earlier. “Let’s go.”
