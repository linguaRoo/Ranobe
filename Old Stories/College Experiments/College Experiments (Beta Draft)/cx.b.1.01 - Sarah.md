title: cx.b.1.01 - Sarah
---

The sun was shining just the way one would hope for on a late August day at the beach, which was less pleasant as Sarah now had to manage about five different pieces of luggage for her new dorm. Looking over the parking lot and seeing hundreds of other barely-adults with their families, she let out a smile. Maybe her darling little brother would be up to helping? “Chaz, could you be a dear and get my suitcase and the books?”

The young man who had been playing on a portable game, looked up and nodded by reflex. “Of course, sis,” he said, walking to the back of the van. “So how was your summer?”

“Huh? Oh, Hawaii was great,” Sarah said, holding out her sleeveless arms to reveal a gorgeous tan. She had gone to Hawaii for some reality TV contest, and it was just such a blur. Some jackass rich boy had won, and she suspected it had been more scripted than the producers let on. She remembered his face clearly enough. He was a slacker, too. He was a bit shorter than her, with brown hair and eyes the color of storm clouds. He got a full ride scholarship and she got… well, she got, what? She got valuable life experience about trusting big-wigs.

“I thought you were going to some place called Angel Wing Island,” her mother said with her eyebrows furrowed. “Or was that just the stage name?”

“Stage name,” Sarah said dismissively. She held up a backpack full of her most personal supplies for university, secrets she would never show her parents, and secured it on her shoulder. Then, she picked up a laptop case with the brand new Apple computer her dad had insisted was a better fit than a Windows PC. Finally, she picked up a duffel bag full of various odds and ends she wanted to have on hand, but which weren’t too very valuable that she couldn’t risk losing them. “I’ll head in and get up to my dorm,” she said. “I wonder if my roomie is already there.”

Sarah started walking and twisted suddenly to the left as she felt the sensation of a skateboarder about to hit her from the sidewalk. Somehow, her intuition was right and a young man with his head shaved, and no helmet, blew right by throwing her the bird and yelling something about watching her step. She didn’t remember when she’d developed this particular awareness, but she did know it was useful.
