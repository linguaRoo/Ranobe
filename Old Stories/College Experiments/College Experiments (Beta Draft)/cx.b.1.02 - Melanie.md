“Josiah, stop messing with my hair,” Melanie said, rolling her eyes. Shen the pressure on the back of her scalp didn’t subside after another five seconds, Melanie let out a whine. “Mo~om, make Josiah leave my hair alone! Why did we even bring him?”

Her step-mother, Dorothy, looked back from the passenger seat of the mini-van and stared past Melanie to the ten-year old boy in the back seat. “Josiah Allen Dijkstra, stop playing with your sister’s hair. 

Melanie had been the victim of this stare, and tone of voice, in the past, with her big sister and brother always finding something to blame her for when they were the ones fighting. Still, she was just moving to dorms that were literally in their own city. She didn’t get why he’d had to come along. If her parents could have afforded a car for her, she’d probably be staying at home.

“Which lot did they say to park in,” her father asked as Melanie realized they were now in all but a gridlock.

“Off Knollview,” Dorothy said with a reassuring tone. Her eyes flicked back to the boy in the back and she narroed them. “Josiah, I see your hands, hold them under your bottom until we are parked!”

Melanie couldn’t see what Josiah was doing, but at least he wasn’t trying to braid her pigtails anymore. Or whatever he’d been doing. After a short time, they managed to park, and Melanie rushed out the sliding door of the minivan as soon as she could. Finally, after 18 years, she got a chance to really be on her own!

“Don’t forget your sunglasses, Melanie,” she heard her mother saying from behind her. “I can see the case sitting in the middle of the seat still!”

Melanie rolled her eyes as she turned around and reached back to grab the glasses case and her handbag. She made sure the handbag was still zipped, then put in in place over her shoulder. With both items secured, she figured it was probably a bright enough day to put her sunglasses on. She just wished her mother had let her pick something more stylish than the aviators that nearly matched the size and shape of her regular glasses.

There was a rush of students getting moved in, and Melanie just wanted to hurry to the dorm. “I need to keep an eye on Josiah,” Dorothy said with a nasal quality as she whined. “David, help Melanie with her bags. Josiah, stay put until your sister has her things out the trunk.”

Melanie grumbled silently at how useless her step-mother was. Sure, she knew Dorothy was Josiah’s birth mother, and shed known Dorothy longer than her own mother by now, but Dorothy always had a habit of treating Melanie like the one child who could never do anything right. Calvin and Martha had always been able to get Melanie blamed, and while Dorothy tended to take Melanie’s side when Josiah picked on her, that was only if it involved actually touching her.

“Thanks for everything,” Melanie muttered as she stood around the back of the trunk. Once her dad got there to open it, he paused and offered her a hug.

“Look, I know you and your mom don’t get along all the time, but she got you this computer for your program, didn’t she?”

Melanie bit her lip and hugged her father. She didn’t get what he really saw in Dorothy, but it wasn’t her place to try and put a wedge between them. “I guess. I just… I want to feel like she’s really my mom, but she treats the twins more like her own than she ever did me. And Josiah too, as long as he isn’t touching me.”

David sighed as Melanie stepped away from the hug. “I can’t make her love you, dear. But she does care and she is proud of you. I’ll help you get moved in so you can keep up the killer work, champ.”

Melanie grabbed a luggage set and smiled. “I’ll head in while you get the computer stuff out, dad.” She started making her way to the crosswalk when she heard someone yelling about watching where someone was going, and then saw a young man with a shaved head go by on a skateboard.
