# A Glossary of Atrusian Literary Terms

The glossary uses a strictly lower-case format for words being defined and identified. They should be properly capitalized when used as proper nouns.

This glossary is a work in progress and is not currently a complete list of all terms used in the Atrusian literary industry: there are bound to be genres and target demographics that exist within the world that have not been documented at present.

Where not otherwise noted, these terms are in the _Adinjo Journalist_ language.

## \#

- **\#** or **\#+**: a numerical indicator of the specific target age minimum, such as 15 or 15+ for stories targeting young adults with a few years of adult life already lived); may use a dash-separated value to identify a specific target age range, such as _genti 6-8_ to indicate a story targeted for children ages 6-8 (roughly equivalent to 8-12 on Gaea)
- **c\#**: indicates a specific chapter or issue of a story or publication; for example, _c15_ refers to “chapter 15” of a particular story; 0-padding of numbers is common, so _c01_ is typical for stories that are still under 100 chapters
- **i\#** or **\#i\#:** literally “of \#,” indicates the length of a story or arc or the specific chapter within an arc, for example _i4_ indicates a 4-chapter arc, while _1i4_ indicates the first chapter of a 4-chapter arc; this is often used more in discussion or re-publication than initial publication (as revealing the length of a story arc in advance could spoil the flow for live readers!)

## A

- **akendan:** epic adventure
- **albor:** ink, paint, pigment; as a genre, refers to strongly-inked black and white style meten
- **apoxendan:** chronicle, record book; literally “era-book”
- **axen:** the very last, ultimate; cf. _xen_

## C

- **carmilé:** growing up, coming of age; a genre usually targeted at audiences from roughly ages 8-14, though some nostalgic _carmilé_ stories are written for older adult audiences
- **chanjiabi:** weekly
- **chennur:** school, academy, university; as a genre, this covers schooland guild-focused stories at all levels of education
- **chimaru:** history, historical; as a genre, this refers to stories set in real historical places, societies, or times, or in (ideally) accurate recreations of such other times and places
- **cori:** some, several, a few; may be prefixed to many terms to indicate a vague but generally small quantity
- **coritan:** anthology story, compilation, collection, omnibus
- **coritwa:** anthology book, anthology magazine

## D

- **dandé:** circle, coterie, team; a small organization of individuals who share copyright on one or more projects
- **delve:** (alternatives include _dig-run_, _dive_, or _exploration_) to enter a dungeon as a form of monster control and often to seek out wealth or rare magical ingredients; (story) a chapter or arc of a _dungeon delver_ story; the term and its alternatives provided here are English translations
- **dobrau:** workplace, employment; as a genre, this refers to stories set primarily in workplaces exploring the skills and relationships of coworkers
- **domyatan:** household stories, family stories; a genre usually preoccupied specifically with the lives of _noble_ households, though the definition is broad enough that any household able to afford resident servants typically qualifies as _noble_ for the genre
- **domyau:** domestic life, community; a genre primarily focused on realistic lifestyle scenarios
- **dul:** big, grand, great, more
- **dungeon:** usually enclosed biomes with high concentrations of mana that can produce monsters through various means, dungeons must generally be maintained by regular adventurer activity to keep the populations from growing large enough to threaten civilization, dungeons may be natural or artificial environments; this term is an English translation
- **dungeon delver:** a genre of stories featuring real or (usually) fictional explorers of dungeons; this term is an English translation

## F

- **failetan:** love story, a genre of stories focused on intimate emotional relationships, usually a combination of familial and platonic emotional intimacy rather than “romance”
- **furatan:** erotica, prurient stories

## G

- **genduli:** older children’s stories, stories for children from 6-8 Atrusian years old (about 8-12 on Gaea)
- **gendyuri:** eldest children’s stories, stories for children from about 8-12 Atrusian years old (about 11-18 on Gaea)
- **genti:** children’s stories, (broad) stories for children from 3-12 Atrusian years old (about 4-18 on Gaea)
- **gentali:** young children’s stories, stories for children from 3-6 Atrusian years old (about 4-8 on Gaea)
- **gogent:** young adult, teenager, post-child; roughly ages 12-23 (roughly ages 17-35)
- **gogental:** transitional young adult, adolescent, “teenager”; roughly ages 9-13 (about 13-19 Gaean years old)
- **gogenti:** stories written for audiences from about 12-23 (about 17-35 on Gaea), living through their second zenade of life
- **gogentali:** transitional young adult, stories written for what Gaeans would recognize as teenagers, roughly ages 9-13 (about 13-19 on Gaea)

### H

- **hauket:** partner-love, the sort of love that expresses itself in any combination of platonic, romantic, and sexual ways
- **hauktan:** romance stories, stories exploring _hauket_ relationships

## I

- **incidental audience:** the audience of a story that is not the deliberately targeted market, but engages with it all the same, such as boys reading a girls story in an all-audiences magazine like _Everybody Read_, or parents reading stories to or for their children; in addition to the primary common use, it is sometimes used as a euphemism for the specific adolescent and adult audience which brings prurient interests to innocent children’s stories (compare this euphemistic usage with Gaean concepts like the “otaku market”); this term is an English localization

## J

- **jiabi:** (frequency) daily; (genre) everyday, slice-of-life
- **jenlintan:** autobiography, memoir, personal recollections; literally “_life-self-story_”
- **jentan:** biography, personal history; literally “life-story”
- **jocon:** adventure, quest, saga
- **joncanbi:** third-weekly

## K

- **kaman:** war, warfare, military battle
- **kamani:** stories focused around warfare and battles, often told from the elevated perspective of military command or commanders
- **kangraen:** political, government, diplomatic
- **kangrau:** city life, urban
- **keldin:** superman, superhuman, superbeing; superhero; literally “super-person”
- **kendan:** legend, tale
- **knight:** a hero or champion who represents some greater cause such as an ideal or a nation-state; a battle-sport athlete who engages in formalized (often international) arena battles that have replaced large-scale international warfare over the past several centuries since the Adin arrival; this is an English translation of the terms used in various languages for heroic warriors and battle-sport athletes
- **kosimet:** eight-panel comic; literally “eight images”
- **kosijan:** eight-story anthologies; literally “eight stories”

## L

- **lekake:** fight, conflict, battle; as a genre, stories that focus on engaging in martial arts, duelling, or other formal small-scale battles (but not typical team sports)
- **luando:** month; Atrusian months are always 44 days long, except every third December
- **luandobi:** monthly
- **lucanbi:** fourth-weekly; three times every two months
- **lunch scene:** a common scene in all sorts of stories where the characters eat lunch and chat during a formal lunch break (usually, this is very low stakes and develops the world of the story); in school settings, this may happen in a cafeteria, classroom, or elsewhere on the school grounds, this scene usually develops school plots or relationships; for a very cozy feel, it may even be set at a character’s home during an extended midday lunch period, usually to develop topics unrelated to school or school relationships; this term is an English translation
- **luxamet:** four-panel comic; literally “four images”
- **luxatan:** four-story anthologies

## M

- **meten:** comic, manga, illustrated story; literally “illustration”
- **mythos:** the “multiverse” of expressions of a concept or idea, across genres and details, such as the “Bal Keldin mythos” which expresses every iteration of the “Bal Keldin” archetype; something akin to the modern English “franchise” of a character; this term is an English translation

## N

- **naiti:** girl, girls’ stories, stories intended for girls, broadly girls from 4-12 Atrusian years old
- **neni:** boys’ stories, stories intended for boys, broadly boys from 4-12 Atrusian years old

## O

- **orksploitation:** a derogatory term for media that features exclusively or primarily orkish characters and plays into their typically most negative stereotypes; (slang) a derogatory term for “gratuitous” orkish characters in any media; this term is an English term and is a play on _blacksploitation_, a parallel genre in 20th century US media

## P

- **paure:** betting, gambling; (independent) stories about gamblers or gambling; (subgenre) the subgenre of stories in which games with high stakes are played, relative to the scale and seeming scope of the story
- **peranti:** (phrase) if you please, may I, as you wish; (style) teaser, preview, advanced release; (genre) fluffy romance, especially of upstairs-downstairs conventions; (fandom & criticism) fanservice
- **preda:** sport, game, puzzle; the broad genre of stories relating to non-military conflict resolution
- **preda genti:** children’s game, youthful play; the genre of stories exploring the playing of children’s game, imagination and roleplaying, and (usually) low-stakes play
- **preda leka’e:** battle sport, combat sport; the genre of stories exploring sports based on or utilizing genuine interpersonal or inter-team combat, such as boxing, wrestling, or gladiatorial sports

## R

- **risheru:** pastoral, farming, ranching; rural or small-town; literally “farming”

## S

- **standard:** (short for _standard length_) a story that runs a standard length; this is a localized usage of the English term
	- 37-38 chapters for literature (about the length of a novel)
	- 144 issues for folio meten (regardless of frequency)
	- 300 chapters for _canjiabi_ anthology meten
	- 150 chapters for _tomolarnjiabi_ meten

## T

- **takama:** nothing, emptiness, void; a scene that emphasizes that despite all of the tension in the story, the world continues on, most often placed at the height of conflict within a story, right between preparation for “battle” and the “battle” itself
- **tal:** short, small, little, lesser
- **tan:** story, tale
- **tantal:** short story, vignette, mini-story; a story that usually fits on 1-4 pages, or in 2000 words or less, sometimes included in _meten wintan_ as bonus content to the illustrated story
- **twa:** book, folio, magazine
- **tomolarnjiabi:** fortnightly; literally “fourteen-daily”, when a publication runs on this schedule, it publishes every fourteen days, meaning that if the weeks shift between new years, it will generally remind readers of the change for several issues prior to the end of the year (formerly _larcanbi_, updated terminology)

## W

- **walu:** evil, dark, twisted; as a genre, stories that follow a dark, anti-social, or villain protagonist
- **wintan:** folio, novel; literally “one-story” refers to a publication with only one primary story
- **wintwa:** single book, standalone book

## X

- **xánen:** magic boy; a genre similar to _xáiti_ or Gaean _magical girl_ stories in which a young boy protagonist learns the power of friendship, emotions, and connections in an empowering masculine manner as a part of growing up
- **xánen’tal:** young magic boy; the subgenre of _xánen_ targeted at teaching the importance of healthy emotional expression to young boys, often with lower or more local stakes than the similar _xáiti’tal_ genre
- **xáiti:** magic girl; the broad genre of stories featuring feminine heroes who use magic as part of the struggles of growing up, whether that is for the sake of learning, developing connections, or fighting evil
- **xáiti’tal:** young magic girl; the subgenre of _xáiti_ targeted at empowering young girls, often focused more on cute aesthetics than on beauty or gracefulness
- **xen:** fin; the end; final, last
- **xoltwa:** journal, diary; travelogue; literally “journal” or “diary”

Note: As a general rule, stories which do not specify a target age group are intended to appeal to either adults _or_ to general audiences of all ages
