Baeron Lassalen is the famed “fourth author” of the Bal Keldin mythos who ran three back to back to back series in the Silver Age, and then retired from primary meten writing and illustration to focus on his passion for writing childrens’ stories. He has always had a soft spot for children, and due to feeling that many children’s books talked down to him, he has wanted to make stories for children that avoid doing that.

Since retiring from his original meten run, he has become a profound advocate of pacifism, garbling recognition, and a very skilled watercolor painter.

## Summaries

We will save this page for summaries of stories which do not have their own dedicated pages.

### Garbling Rukib (The Curious Garbling)

This is a series of children’s books that is viable for children from 3-8, balancing the idea of learning about the world, society, and reading and language skills. It is also an activist stance taken by Lassalen, who has stated that he originally intended to use a different primitive creature, but in his research period became convinced that it was very important to support the growing body of scientific research that Garblings are actually a sapient species that simply has a different approach to culture than other species. The series has been one of the most successful propaganda campaigns to date in getting people to view Garblings as mere “annoyances” rather than pests or natural disasters.

The initial three books cover the life of a naturally living Garbling as it explores its world, with the third finally introducing the character of the Greentender, a Kenyu who comes to understand the Curious Garbling, and offers to be responsible for helping the Garbling learn about human culture. The Garbling is typically portrayed as haping about a 3-4 year old child’s level of understanding about the world, and only occasionally uses fairly simple human language (though when interacting with other Garblings, it seems capable of being more expressive).

### Keldin Adventures: Shield and Spear

For a brief two-year span, Lassalen returned to to the story of two superheroes, a large, bulky, armored humanoid calling themself “Shield” and their small, hyperactive, Garbling companion, “Spear.” The two call themselves “Keldin of the Wild” and have an intensely supportive combat style. Some _Bal Keldin_ fans believe this story is set in the same version of Atrusius as the previous _Keldin Adventures_ series, but it is highly debated as part of the broader _Bal Keldin_ mythos. It does, however, use magic crystals as power sources and currency, and has a similar vibe to _Bal the Mercenary,_ even to those who dismiss it from the _Bal Keldin_ mythos.

The overarching plot of the series actually focuses on the economics of being _keldin_ adventurers who go on digruns to keep dungeons from growing too large, but needing to invest most of their income on maintaining their lifestyle, their armor, and their weapons. Spear is a very eloquent Garbling who performs all of the maintenance work on their equipment, while Shield does most of the maintenance on their camp gear and cooking for them.

The story ends in a sort of endless everyday for adventuring: while they secure a significant payday from clearing a dungeon, they also need to replenish so many supplies that they’re no closer than retiring than when they began, but they’ve at least earned the respect of the city they just protected.

### Illustrated Myths

While not a traditional series, Lassalen has worked to produce a number of large-page format illustrated books of Atrusian mythology at the sweet spot of simplicity and complexity for children. Each book covers 3-4 myths in detail, with several full-page illustrations for each story. The series is extremely well received even by adult experts in storytelling and mythology.

### Kenyu Fairy Stories

Similar to Illustrated Myths, this is Lassalen’s second return to anthology publishing after Cyber Soldier. Published in _Everybody Read_, Lassalen used the framing of a group of four (later five) children discovering a magical folder that holds nearly empty parchment pages, but is meant to contain all of the fairy stories of their village. The only text that remains at the start is the titles, and in every several chapters, they experience the plot of a fairy story in order to fill the pages back in. These stories are updated and modernized for the 340—350 era, using modern contemporary language, technology, and coatings over the classical framework. Over the course of the 4 year weekly project, Lassalen covers fairy stories like _The Two Smiths, The Queen and the Traveler, The Princess of the Flowers,_ and _The Fairy Bell._

At the end of the series, the book is full, and the children return the fully completed book to a safe resting place in the library.

### Magical Doppelgänger: 24-year Anniversary Story

As part of a compilation book focusing on the key points in the narrative of _Magical Doppelgänger_, Lassalen wrote a 30-page special story set just between the Second and Third years of the story, outlining the growth of Bal’s ability to synthesize powers together and to maintain powers for longer, including some training scenes with Burmon and Anesh, as well as Tembar helping Liyer and Luko engineer supporting equipment that debuted during the Third year, including (most notably) Liyer’s Wind Blade Bracers which allowed her to fight at Bal’s side and had originally come seemingly out of nowhere in the plot. The story ends with Bal complimenting Liyer on how quickly she was taking to swordplay, and assuring her that she’s always a big help: on the battlefield or at home.

### Naldowlikandon

A brand new _akendan_ put together by a circle of Farlannan Kenyu who approached Lassalen to be the illustrator for a cultural creativity and preservation project. This series of six novels tells the story of Leng (a name which means “Winter”), a 9-years-old Kenyu from the southern reaches of Farlann’s forests, where he was raised primarily among the Peratan. When a dark curse is placed over his village, Leng is summoned by the Guardian Beast of the Forest. He is told that his family came here to flee war when he still an infant, and that the Guardian Beast sensed a great Power of Destiny within him -- so he has protected the youth until now, but the curse can only be broken if Leng follows the course of that destiny.

Over the six books, Leng is challenged to trials in the Forest, Rocklands, Sacred Fountain, Desert Waste, Dead Marsh, and Caverns of Flame, overcoming each with his wits and tools found through his travels. At the Sacred Fountain, he meets the Princess of Farlann, who reveals that she too holds a great Power of Destiny, and that the two of them must proceed together through the remaining regions of the kingdom so that they may find and overcome the Third Power, restoring balance between the Gods of Earth, Wind, and Water. At the end of the Caverns of Flame, Leng’s sword is imbued with holy light from the Gods of Wind and Water, but before the God of Earth can grant a blessing, the Third Power reveals himself, a towering Ork warlord who kidnaps the Princess.

In the final book, Leng must earn the blessing of the God of Earth to finish empowering his blade, returning to each of the trials he has already overcome to free the spirits who had been sealed away by the Third Power. Only after restoring all six spirits can the God of Earth finish blessing the Sword of Three Powers, allowing Leng to break the barrier the Third Power erected around the castle. The Third Power has turned from balance to powerlust, and so Leng is told his name, and told to speak it to strip the Third Power of his blessing after overcoming him in battle. The battle is hard fought, but Leng achieves victory and speaks the name “Yanodorin,” drawing a sacred pearl of destiny out of the Third Power. The Princess and Leng then release their own sacred pearls, allowing them to scatter the power of destiny to restore hope to the land.

### Naldwolikandon: Light of the Gods

_This is but one of the legends the people of Farlann tell…_

This standalone illustrated novel tells another story about Farlann, in a different time long before Yanodorin was born or became the Third Power. It tells a simpler legend, of a Princess simply named Sraldi, meaning “daughter,” and how she became the First Power with the aid of a knight—who became the Second Power—named Raenga, meaning “moon.” While they were unable to claim the Third Power, they still created a blessing upon the lands of Farlann that ensured its flourishing for many millenia, until the rise of an Orkish invader…

### Naldowlikandon: Leng’s Shadow

This meten was published weekly over two years, and explores the aftermath of the original _Naldwolikandon,_ as Leng struggles with darkness growing in his own soul. Finding the Sacred Spring, he excises his doubts, but those doubts become a shadow of Yanodorin and steal the Third Power from its resting place deep within the spring. This story explores the duality of mortal beings, even the most noble and heroic, and requires Leng to master weilding not only the blessing of Wind, but the Blessing of Water, to find balance within himself in order to overcome his own dark shadow.

### The Merchant and the Familiar

While traveling across the Jargyn Desert, a traveling merchant by name of Darmi finds a stone circle and hears a voice calling out for help. Despite the protests of his caravan, he pauses to investigate. Carefully examining the circle, he applies a slight magical pressure and a familiar spirit pops out of the ring. It introduces itself as Jonnae, and the merchant introduces himself. She asks to learn about the mortal world from him, and in exchange, she will be his companion for life. Darmi hesitates and says that contracts with spirits are frowned upon in this age, for it is said they always end is tears. Jonnae nods and says she is not seeking a contract. She has been sealed for ages, and her last contract did end in sorrow, so she is only seeking company and knowledge. Darmi reluctantly agrees, but they both agree that they would do best to never hold hands.

Over the course of three years, Lassalen uses the story to develop a rich explanation of modern Atrusian economics, trade routes, and even multicultural interactions that is catered surprisingly well to the adolescent target audience, with a surprisingly charming aesthetic combining strong linework for the physical world and mortals with almost ethereal artwork for Jonnae in her spirit form as a _lyscil_ and her humanoid form as an ork.
