# The Record of Belthak

written by General Bel’thak Org’ÿllÿs

## I

_Many people think that war died 300 years ago. That all of the peoples of this world have been peaceful and unified since then. They couldn’t be more wrong, but the special forces are the reason it seems that way. There are twelve divisions of nine members each, code named for the greatest beasts of the world. I was originally assigned to Rknary Division, the lowest division, but in time I worked through the ranks to become the general of Worg Division, and alpha general of the Special Forces Elite Divisions \-- Drakyr, Worg, Tsellon and Jagnel._  

_These are my memoirs, the memoirs of General Belthak Org’yllys. Contained in them are missions once held confidential, but before I die each and every one will be declassified by the Imperial Adin High Command due to a statute of limitations. Many of them are already open._  

_This is my story. This is my battle._  

_This is my life._

I attended a brutish combat school in my childhood, learning the ways of the classical raiding party, the use of a worg stick, and even dueling practices. But nothing I had lived through in the hellish flatlands of Hao’gnerk was enough to prepare me for boot camp. I was sixteen, as were many of the other recruits. Sure, there were older elves and dwarves, but it seemed late adolescence brought us here. Not yet adults, the Imperial Army would make us soldiers worthy of keeping the peace in our respective homelands. Four hundred of us were led into Fort Stonewright after our induction ceremony. They said only one hundred would survive the first week without scars.  

They were right. For the first week we lived on scarce rations, with barely time to eat and none to socialize. It was only on the first seven-day we were given any sort of reprieve. “Get to know your unit,” the commander, an aged elf, told us after the rolls were called. “Assignments are posted on the barracks doors, in mess, and on the message board at the front of these parade grounds. Get to know them, they will be the ones who save your life here.”  

At this we were dismissed to leisure, and I took my leave of the parade grounds without a glance at the posted lists. In those days I had little concern for others, and felt I could pull through anything by the grit of my teeth and strength of my blood alone. I spent the day taking advantage of the leisure, until a dwarf came up to me.  

“Excuse me,” he said softly. I saw fear, and I seized on the opening.  

“Leave me be,” I said harshly, adopting the classical swooping hawk position.  
He blanched as he saw me acting aggressive. For his lack of a face, what there was seemed as white as it could be. “N… No. Me name, me name is Tedz’mai.” Though he started nervous, I could hear his voice becoming harder than a gratshrin ax, and his eyes become like iron. “The list, it say ye’s in me unit. So me’s here to meet the ork who will be savin’ me’s life. Ye are Orgillys, right?”  

I hesitated. Tedzumai was a name I had heard. He’d always seemed to be one of the dwarves behind the curve this past week, and to think he was in my unit? Was I with the dregs? I loosened my stance and turned my face away. “Sorry, short one. But I don’t think I got much use for a unit. I can make it through on my own.”  

“N… No. Ye’s seen the way they’s pushin’ us.” His voice had a resolve to it now. Something that made me keep listening. “They’s pushin’ us to the end o’ our own tunnels. To the bottom of the hole, y’know? Maybe ye’s a lucky one. Maybe ye can live on shortbread and dry berries for the next alf-year.” It was here that he paused. I turned to look at him again, and saw his face. He didn’t need to finish. His face told me more than his next words: “But not me. Me’s already gettin’ down to that end, Orgillys. Me’s already past the point of turnin’ back, too. Me leave here and me’s name turns to Menku.”  

“And what makes you think I give a bloody…”  

“Because me sees yer eyes.” He looked up at me. “Me’s family be smiths. Real smiths, mind ye, not yer surface-dwellin’ woodsmiths. Me can make a sword or an ax, mind ye, and do a better jobba it than yer stonework too. Y’call that gratshit an ax, but me don’t. Me’s family makes real axes. Mithral steel. Iron. Brinze. Yah, we makes the real stuff.”  

He insulted my people and our heritage with such vehemence I was about to leave. “But we doesn’t make strong ‘arts. Strong body, but no good at the ‘art. We does what ye’s doin. We shun the other families. Mine our own metal, make our own deals for mithral. Me had t’fight to even enlist with the imperials, and me’s as much as disowned if me don’ make a commission. Tha’s why me’s name is good as Menku if me can’t shape up, and tha’s why me came to ye.”  

The passion he spoke with was fierce. Perhaps that was why I stayed. “Yes. I am Org’ÿllys. But the strong heart belies the true strength of character. You say you fought to come \-- of what importance then is your name? Either you will succeed or fail, and there is no middle. But as the old raiders teach, trust your wolf and trust your blade, but trust your victory before all else.” I stared him down. “I hate proverbs, though. Never understand ‘em, they got so many levels. But I was always told the point of that one is you need to believe you’ll make it if you want to.”  

“But it says ye gotta trust yer weapon and steed too,” he said. “The tool and the device carrying ye. In this case those is the rest of our unit. We’s only strong as the weakest ring, and me’s thoughts say me’s the weakest. Ye looked to be strong, so me came to ye for advice.”  

I sat myself down and looked up to the sky. It was a clear day, and this was almost an omen. The scriptures, not that I had ever paid them much attention before, said much about teams. That we were made in the image of the goddess and her brothers, to work as one but not by ones. That no raid was full with but a single raider. That even the beasts work as one in their packs. “I suppose that is my advice, Tedzumai. Believe you’ll make it.”

The weeks went on from that day. Tedzumai and I became friends after a sort. He’d been given the name Keresh, and said it meant “flawless steel”, which I thought was a stupid idea. Upon explaining the name, I had said “Steel is iron mixed with ashes. The ashes are a flaw, even if they do make the iron stronger.”  

“Perhaps that is why the name is considered to be neither good nor bad,” he’d replied. “And yours?”  

“Belthak is the great brakk wolf that watches the world. The name is considered good luck,” I explained. “It’s not a common name, though. I think the legends say his name means ‘two eyes fully open’ or something, to mean that he sees everything, but I know one legend says he was named ‘beloved’ by the goddess. Mixed ideas, which is why I just say it’s his name and leave it at that.”  

A day or two after that, there was an endurance test. Each unit was to compete as a whole, while our previous tests had all been as individuals. And when they led us to the proving grounds, I saw why. There were obstacles none of us could surmount alone. Even the strongest or largest or best of us would be unable to compete alone.  

Yet as a team, we were able to get far beyond what any one of us could do. It was not until the final test, crossing a series of long planks, that our unit found difficulty. Most of our unit was heavier members, and as each of us crossed, the boards groaned. In addition, we needed someone at one end of any given plank to keep it steady, lest we fall off. And below us was a pit of tar.  

We had agreed that I, as the heaviest, would be the last across any segment. It was halfway through that the real trouble began. I could see the plank creaking and bending even from our lightweight, an elf who was still heavy for his height by the name of Delenor. Delenor tended to shun the group most of the time, but wasn’t bad about working together with the team.  

The second across was one of our gramurn teammates. Rabenash nearly slipped as he walked carefully across, trying to keep his weight balanced. He made it on all fours, and anchored the plank at his end for Mayra. With it anchored on both ends, Mayra and the others had little trouble. Tedzumai, however, had never been good at the balance tests, and as he went, just before me, the weak plank gave out. He fell into the tar pool, and the plank was beyond repair. He was unfortunate to be both a dwarf and one of our heavy members, now sinking into a tar pool.  

The proctor was watching us. “Go on,” I shouted to Delenor. “Keresh and I will get there\! Just go on while I get him\!”  

“You’re a fool,” Delenor shouted back. He had also become a de facto leader, being one of the most charming members of the unit. “Just get over here. If you jump you can get to this end of the plank, and we can still finish. We’re all over here pulling for you, Orgillys\!”  

“Not all,” I shouted back. “We can’t just leave him there\!” He had stretched out a hand and it was all that was left above the water. “I’m going to get him out, Delenor.”  

And with that, I jumped into the pool. His hand was still sinking, and I knew I would sink too if I didn’t hurry. Delenor was watching, as was the whole unit. I trudged through the slime and muck to reach my friend, and knew the timer was running all the while. We only had a certain amount of time to finish the trial in, and if even one of us made it to the end late…  

“They didn’t say we fail if he doesn’t make it to the end at all,” Mayra shouted at me. “Just get yourself over here, Orgillys. Get yourself over here so we can still finish\!”  

I seemed to know that leaving a comrade behind was no good way to achieve victory. “I said go without me\! We will make it, team, but I refuse to leave Tedzumai behind\!”  

I was sinking rapidly, and in a moment I could neither see nor hear them. It was like swimming, if you were to swim in molasses. It took all my strength to guide my motions against the downward pull, but I had to find Keresh. I did hit the bottom, and the pool seemed to be only about 10 feet deep. I caught hold of his hand and tugged. I only had a minute or so before I would start losing my own faculties by now, and not much longer before I passed out. Worst of all, he seemed to be stuck. He had been down here longer than me \-- he might already have passed out. I reached for his feet and swung. There was nothing there. I lifted his feet and realized it was his own body weight hindering me, amplified by the consistency of the tar. I turned around and positioned myself to lift him on my back \-- he was still conscious, as he wrapped his arms around my neck without my help.  

I knew I was out of luck in terms of swimming, with him on my back. I tried to orient myself towards the goal… and trudged. It was not easy, and I did come to fear I was near passing out. My eyes closed the whole time, I was seeing dancing lights before I felt my head break the surface. I kept trudging, making sure my mouth was out of the pool before I dared take a deep breath. I raised one hand and brushed my eyes clear of the tar, though the tar-covered hand did a poor job of it, and spotted the goal. I didn’t speak as I carried Keresh to the shore, and onwards towards the goal. I walked in silence across that line before I realized Delenor was still waiting for me in the middle of the trial.  

“Move it, soldiers,” I shouted, crossing the goal line. And with those words out of my lips, I collapsed. Holding my breath and straining my body past the limits had finally taken their toll. But I had done what I needed to.

---

They say I was unconscious for a week. Keresh only for a few hours, and up by dinner, but the medical corps had started to worry about my condition. “Hey there Black,” was the first thing I heard after I opened my eyes.  

Delenor and Rabenash were standing by the foot of my bed, looking at me. To my side was Keresh. Mayra and the others were nowhere to be seen. I learned soon after that they were at drills, and only these three had asked to visit my bed that day. Keresh, it turned out, had been there daily. Mayra had been there as well, and had even left a few flowers tied together with a silver ribbon. I didn’t know then that those flowers were a symbol of honor for a hero, and could be considered a courtship gift. That did, once I learned it, explain the irritation Rabenash showed when I looked at the flowers.  

“Me told ye,” Keresh said, pounding one open hand onto my chest. “Me told ye, that ye would be the one what saves me’s life. We’s brothers now, Orgillys. We’s brothers in arms and in facing down death herself.”  

“Himself,” I muttered.  

“Black.” I turned from Keresh to look up at Delenor. He was big, he was smart, and the only thing he seemed to lack was a head for good words. “When I heard you… I turned and saw you fall down, but all I saw was Black. I was like, ‘Even the blackness?’ before Rab told me it was you. You were different then. You were the leader when you said that.” He shook he head. “I’m sorry Orgillys. Sorry I pushed you to… General Theras heard about what you did, and, he wants you to report to him as soon as you’re discharged. II think you’re going to be promoted. I think you’ll officially be the unit leader.”  

“He’s prob’ly right,” Rabenash said. “You were in charge there… and even with you unconscious, Delenor couldn’t stop staring at you. The two of us followed the medics until they said we couldn’t go any further. It’s been a rough week without you, Black. We didn’t realize how much you pulled all of us through the drills, and how important it was to have nine members.”  

The two of them left after that. Keresh stayed behind though. “They’s all been calling ye Black,” he said softly. “Because of the tar. Because of what it looked like. They’s not meanin’ an insult… it’s an honor-name. Ye earned it for saving me, Belthak. And for that, me’s never gonna forget about ye.”  

I nodded and didn’t speak. There was nothing to say. I had simply done the right thing, saving a friend and a comrade. I’d had no doubts in my mind it was right, and I still believe I would do that for any of my teammates at any time in my career.  

I was promoted for my valor to Private First Class, and given field command over my unit. We pushed through the remainder of our basic training for the next two months, and were one of the top units at graduation. Not only that, Keresh and I were the two top graduates from our entire entry division. He received honors for most improved soldier, while I received honors for valor, dedication and self-sacrifice. Our entire unit was promoted to Sergeant rank, and I to Sergeant First Class \-- head of our unit in advanced training. But that is another story for another time.

The legend of the Howling Brakks is still passed down among recruits at Fort Forgewright, and those of us still alive even meet up in Orinthia, once a year. Lieutenant Tedzumai, Lieutenant Corporal Mayra and Colonel Grellen even keep in regular correspondence with me. However, none of them ended up in the special forces. And the special forces are where the next major chapter of my life occurred.

## II

_Hao’gnerk is a hellish place even in good years, and I grew up during rough times. For a few years as a babe, Hao’gnerk was in good condition, growing sufficient grains and fruits to keep the citizens well fed, and still there was surplus. However the soils of the region are weak, and the droughts that came in when I was five left the entire town devastated. At eight we fought both the drought and a plague, and nearly half of the children died._

_Those of us who survived were toughened from it by the end of the droughts, in 295, by the time my twelfth year began. I was born in 283 by the Adin Reckoning, and it was not until twelve that my childhood peers and I were enrolled in combat school. This was the place where I learned the basics of fighting with a blade for two harsh years, as well as the rules of engagement in battle, in raiding, and in duels. It was also around the time that the droughts ended when Hao’gnerk re-instituted sporting events, from games to hunts._

I awoke on the first day of combat school like any other day for the yers before or the years after. I knew the day would be difficult, of course, and stopped by the temple before school. While I was never deeply religious, I do recognize that appeasing the spirits is usually a fortuitous course of action. I arrived at combat school before most of the other students. I did not have any desire to make friends here.  

We were taught by my uncle, Gruflag, in the morning. He taught us the basics of engagement, and that you must adjust your tactics by the size of your force and the enemy force. Anybody foolish enough to fight an army with the same strategy as a single unit or a lone foe as if he were an army was a fool. These morning lessons would give us the knowledge to know what strategy to use in any situation. But the overview her gave on the first day was dull.  

We were given a recess at midday for a meal, and most of the students went home. My parents were there and asked how the lecture had been. “Dull. I thought combat school was where we learn to fight,” I’d said, taking a plate of beef and tubers. “I’d sooner live the lessons than hear uncle explain everything so… so stiffly.” Walking back from the kitchen to our common room, I set myself down at the table and waited for mother and father to take their seats.  

In a moment we were all there. Father asked a blessing over the meal, and when he was done, we ate. After eating, we talked about what we had for the rest of the day. Mother would return to the family shop for her afternoon duries while father remained home to tend the house and kitchen. This was the manner in which our family divided labor to ensure each was pulling their weight in the community as well as the house, and when I had not been in school, I had done my part in both jobs as well, most often with father.  

My afternoon consisted of an introduction to martial tactics, including drilling us on the proper stances for offense, defense and even how to breathe and stand to retain good posture. While other races find our combat school strange and harsh, this was how all young members of the orkish people were raised in my childhood, and how many strict orkish communities still raise the young.  

On the first day, there were no opportunities to meet other students or to socialize. Not until sundown when we were sent home did any of us have a chance to speak.

The second day was very similar. However in the morning, Gruflag began our lessons with a tale of battle, and he told it with a passion that belied his status as an archivist, and made me think of a true keeper of our legends. He then explained that this tale from the time of his grandparents’ grandparents was a family tale, about the raid in which our ancient patriarch had first met his mate, and that though he alone had survived the raid from his own side, the village he had raided saw him as a hero and offered him anything if he would stop the attack.  

This, he explained, was because this ancestor had wisely changed his strategy when he had realized the willage was well guarded, while the rest of his force still suspected and behaved as though it would be a simple battle against only a few guards. He had taken out most of the enemy forces on his own with a hefty axe and his own worg stick.  

He then began explaining with professional passion and tenacity that most combat we would likely encounter was of the single opponent variety, which would mean we treated our opponent as an equal and fought him as one. “You must never, at any time, assume your opponent is any less skilled than you,” he shouted repeatedly during the lesson.  

At the end of the lesson, he gave a sheet of paper to each of us with his own sage words of advice on it. “If you ever treat an opponent as anything but your equal, err cautiously and assume him your superior.”  

He stood before the youth of Hao’gnerk and barked out an attention. We all stood straight and tall, facing him. “Beginning tomorrow you will be assigned training partners and will be taught the actual combat styles used in single combat. For the next month you will leave this school twice a day worn from combat, and then, if you are lucky, we will not need to return to lectures.”  
Afternoon again saw us training in the primitive basics of martial tactics. Stances, attention, at ease, bearing… crops to the neck, or any other offending part of our posture, kept us aware of where we each needed to focus our efforts. I was one of the least injured come day’s end, but my arms still received their share of welts from the crop.  

Indeed, the entire week was much the same. Those of us who had made it to the school already knew how to handle single combat well, and it was only the battles against our partners that made the days seem the least bit fun. School, we were told, was to train us, to make us strong so that when we were of age, we would contribute fully to the community.  

The next week we were kept in combat school for the full day for our lessons. The teaching staff had lunches set out in the courtyard area for us so we could meet, and actually get to know, our classmates. This was not so easy as it might have been had we lived in an Adin settlement, or even one where things had been going well for the past eight years. All of us were greedy, pragmatic, cynical: in short, we were not eager to make friends, let alone the ngarak we would be if we ever worked to defend the town or to raid another.  
Beyond this, the elder students and the younger did not wish to interact much. They didn’t trust us, and we were too concerned with ourselves. In the end, a few circles did form, but almost every group had no more than a year’s age variance among members.  

I was not part of one. From time to time I would eat with my partner, but even as a child I was called the brakk of the village, because I did things alone.

---

The first notable lesson came a few months into combat school, when we were set to enter team combat, working with our partner against another team. By this point, I knew Fergul, my partner, well in a combat sense. He was not a primary combatant, he was a plotter. He always looked to the dirt, never seeing the sky, and rarely the bodies of others, yet his plans were sound. His bearing was loose and weak, however, and in combat it was rare for him to perform better than me.  

The thing was, at this point we had been taught only single battle tactics, and as teams were called, I was nervous but also eager to prove myself.  

Fergul was bearing himself differently today, though. “I may be the weak one, but I don’t want to disappoint you, mother,” he said, looking upwards. This was not Fergul as I knew him, looking up and standing tall. It made me curious about him and his family, for they were a mystery to me.  

“Team thirteen, team fifteen,” the teacher called out. Our combat partnership was team thirteen, and so we approached the ring and watched as our opponents came. Our opponents were both larger girls, taller by a head than myself. I vaguely recognized them as twins, around my own age, but knew little else of them. “Belthak arrived first. Team thirteen, take your position.”  
This was something we had known already from other exercises. The first student to arrive acted as defender during these exercises, which in itself led to a different tactic, and so our team would be serving to defend the position.  

“Fergul, I know you always lose, but please just try to get this one right,” I growled at him as we got into position.  

As I looked at him there was no smirk, no smile… just a determined fierceness set over his entire face. “Just try to keep up,” he said.  

“Kerra and Varlaan, approach on the count.”  

I watched them, to see what they would do. Certainly Fergul looked to be in a different frame of mind, but that did not mean he would be useful. If I were left with two opponents, I would need to fight differently. The scrapes we once had in the streets as children had almost always been ring of trials battles, like this, with one enemy. One of us trying to defend himself from a tougher or better fed or older child. I had seen times where the bullish types had teamed up on defenseless children, but this was my first time in a double combat situation, with or without a partner. I was concerned.  

And as quickly as that, the countdown was over and they were charging in. I saw that the more slender of the two was slightly ahead of, and could possibly trip up, her sister. I heard Fergul shifting his stance, his left foot sliding backwards. But the larger twin was on his side, and the small one on mine. I raised my arms in a guard and stepped forward, two firm steps. The slender one attempted to twist around me, but made the mistake of moving inwards. This put her in her sister’s way, which meant that as I gripped her wrists… And the larger one couldn’t stop. She tumbled into her smaller sister, and as I released my grip, they fell. They were down, but far from out.  

“Kerra,” the larger one said, her glare rising from her sister to me. “How dare you get in my way?” She was breathing heavily already, her shoulders heaving as she got back up.  

“I’m fine,” Kerra said after tense seconds. “Varlaan, I guess this means we can’t hold back. Figured that Gruntak runt meant we would have an easy win.” She cracked her knuckles and stared at Fergul. Was that why he seemed different? Of course we’d known our opponents before the fight, so maybe that…  

I couldn’t even finish the though before Fergul just landed a punch point on in Kerra’s stomach. Next, he made a strong cross chop that sent her back to the ground, collapsed at my feet. The follow up was a sharp monkey kick, his front foot rising backwards as he turned around, until his heel connected with Varlaan’s chin. The sharp crack of impact left me in shock. “This is the dragon of my family’s honor,” he said. “When your clan reclaims theirs, you ought to consider making reparations.”  

I just stared at Fergul Gruntak with a new respect.

---

Afterwards we were all given reports on our performances. Both Fergul and I had done well, though it did state that neither of us had truly fought as a team or as if we were fighting a team.  

“I don’t need to uphold my honor in lessons,” Fergul explained after school was out that day. “And I have no drive to fight outside of it. But their family insulted mine a generation ago, and they… they have insulted me regularly. I took my time to find a fair opportunity, and exploited their overconfidence.”  
I nodded. “So you just let me win when we spar?”  

“Not exactly. We’ve never needed, in lessons, to use full ferocity, so I was leveling our playing field. My parents say that Thyrk’urna blessed me with the might of the beasts, and so they taught me to restrain myself. I don’t feel it would be honorable to use my full strength in training that does not require it.”  

“And what if I don’t feel it’s honorable to…”  

“Belthak the brakk wolf. The school isn’t about honor, and you would learn nothing by losing because you could not match my gifts.” His voice grew firm as we approached the town well, and he looked towards me. “If you truly wish to see my limits, meet me at the well on Sunday afternoon, fifth watch.”  

That night, I recounted this story to my parents. I was undecided as to if I would visit him on Sunday, for as much as I wished to test myself properly against him, I feared the dishonor of being defeated. My parents remained silent, neither encouraging or discouraging my curiosity. Thursday classes were canceled due to a roving band of garblings which all of the adults went out to hunt or scare off. Friday we were back to single drills. Saturday we were lectured on the importance of teamwork before being launched into double drills. Sunday we went to the temple, made our prayers and offerings, and then I faced my decision.  

At fifth watch, I found myself at the well. Fergul was waiting, and he dragged me away by the wrist as soon as I came near. We left town, into the first isa orchard, and when the trees kept us from seeing town at all, he stopped. “Yram turn not your eyes from your son,” he chanted. “For the might of the beast I shall not flaunt, nor abuse the weak and innocent. My strength is a tool for the benefit of Hao’gnerk, and I shall not squander it. Though I demonstrate, I do not boast, for not of my self, but of you am I blessed.”  

He took several deep breaths after that, and then delivered a cyclone kick to the tree, shattering bark and the trunk for a hand’s width deep. My eyes grew wide in shock at this display of force, but he was not through. “Belthak, watch me and understand why I tame myself among others.” He delivered a flurry of punches to the tree, causing splinters and chips of fresh wood to fly, until the tree was nearly eradicated to the midpoint of its width. And then, he leaped along the path of a long branch, grabbing it while still thick, and with a grunt and a tug, I watched it come apart from the trunk as it were attached with the crudest of sticky-grease.  

I was speechless as he gripped the tree where he had shattered it and pushed it over. “This is not even the greatest extent of my strength,” he said softly, harvesting the nearly ripened fruits of the tree. “This tree was to be culled after the harvesting season, but my parents permitted me to do this today, Belthak. This is my family’s orchard, where we grow the leng’eni for the town, and when we have surplus, we store them for times of need. Little of the work of the Gruntak family leaves Hao’gnerk, but a generation back, the Farot family accused my mother and her parents of providing for garblings and selling the best of their harvest to the Adin. This lie has tainted our reputation among some families, and the insult that we would leave our orchard open to garblings has caused certain people in town to point a finger at us when garbling gangs do become a significant threat. On Wednesday, I repaid the honor of my family against the Farot twins.”  

“Surely this will not change the opinions of people,” I said, a note of unease in my voice. I had always given much thought to things, and his story did not convince me that the town would see his family as honorable simply because he had defeated the Faort family’s children in a schoolyard combat test. “The Farot family will still claim your family to be dishonorable, and their allies will still blame your family for garbling raids. Now they will merely have fuel to add by claiming that you have attacked them to cover up the dishonorable deeds of the Gruntak family.”  

He did not stop his harvest, and remained silent for a moment. When he did speak, I could hear a note of pity in his voice. “Perhaps not, but my parents have worked hard with the council to ensure that it is known the Farot family is dishonoring us with false claims, and they have even discovered the original root of the problem. The Farot family has their own isa orchard a furlong out beyond ours, and my father was once promised to the eldest daughter of the Farot family, for in the last generation, they had no sons who were fit to inherit their orchard. They had hoped, it seems, that by marrying a Gruntak to a Farot, we would become responsible for the orchard leaving them free to settle in the city walls.”  

“However, their firstborn daughter, Erkasa, died two years before the marriage was arranged to occur. The Farot’s expected that due to the wording of the agreement, that my father would marry their eldest daughter, to mean his obligations had transferred to Erkasa’s sister, Ofan, who in our life is head of the Farot household. Grandfather Darkam, however, swore that he had promised his son to the firstborn daughter of the Farots, and the council supported his position that the death of Erkasa nullified the oral contract. They said that because it was oral, his record of meaning was all that could be legally admitted, and that if the Farots wished to ensure such an agreement, they should insist on having a written contract of agreement. After this, my father was given to his cousin, my mother, and the Farots began to claim these lies about our family.”  

I nodded in understanding. The politics were not entirely beyond me, but I had hopes that I was not a mere bargaining chip for my parents to use socially. I went home understanding the story, and fearful of crossing Fergul.

For two years combat school dragged on, but those lessons from my early days were the most powerful reminders of the importance of proper form and understanding. When the day came that I was released from combat school for the last time, I felt a regret that I would not be fighting, but I also knew that as of that day, I was considered an adult.  

For one year, I played cross with my fellow graduates, worked in the family store, and assisted in hunts, one raid, and at the request of Fergul, one isa harvest. I was unsatisfied, however, in this life. It was too calm, too complacent. I hungered for adventure, and with my parents’ blessings, I set out to the south as a wandering adventurer.

## III

_The Thieves’ Guild is not a single organization spanning all of the world, though most thieves would just as soon have their victims think it is, and that anything brought into the Guild will never find its way back to the original owner. Of course, the guilds themselves have tight policies about how much they exist, and that amount is none. Thieves’ guilds, publicly, do not exist, despite the fact that every peasant, merchant, and noble has heard about them._  

_Many of these guilds operate under other names, of course. I was ordered to infiltrate the Silk Washers, a secret organization in Sunbank with rumoured ties to burglars, muggers, and supposedly even assassins. I was given a cover story and identity, and two weeks to prepare. I was reporting to an undercover street operative who gave me the crash course I needed in assuming a cover identity, and then I was given a history._  

_According to the story, I had burgled an apothecary (in Yemaros, a northern city with many Elressie), and the guards were out hunting for me. I had a near brush with them while carrying a load of the potions from the apothecary (all of them illegal prescription pots), and one of them had a wolf (no, I decided to make him a gramurn). Despite nerves and being nearly found out (they had me nearly cornered outside of a tavern), I snaked my way safely through the ordeal (the gramurn had a cold, and I convinced him that if he was smelling anything, it was inside the tavern) before safely meeting a cleaner to get the stuff from me to the people who really wanted it. According to my handler, making this story my own was a key. He had made sure the story wasn’t too detailed, so I need to fill in some blanks, but also that it would be convincing to people who might doubt me…_

_Then, there was the job. I made some noise at a merchant’s house (all staged), took off with a bundle of leather armor parts (legitimately bought, then reported stolen), and had to get into the Silk Washers on my own…_

I ran up to a man in worn out cotton and felt clothing and grabbed him, pulling him into an alley. “I done slipped it big, Dar, I done slipped it big and now I gotta move it,” I said. I was sure he was a thief. For my own purposes, I’d watched him working the town square for two days, and I knew that every chance he got, he’d swipe a ring or a watch along with a purse. I’d also formed a rapport with him for a few days telling about my time cutting purses up north. On top of all that, my intel had initially pointed to him as a way into the Silk Washers. I gripped him by the shirt, desperation on my face, or so I hoped. “I didn’ wanna do it, but me mate got me low in the tav, betted a purse of the old on me not ‘avin’ the beans, and took me out to the tanshop. I know the sniffers are out and the eagles know the mouse. Any way to help a fellow knife lover out?”  

Knowing the cant had been crucial, to make me look like a well established thief. I couldn’t, after all, just say “I need to sell this stolen leather,” or a self respecting thief would just turn me in for a laugh, or at the least think I was a novice.  

“Whatcha gotta tell,” he said, a strange calmness on his face. “Ya tell me whatsup, I tell ya if I can look into the help.”  

“Heavy tans.”  

“I know a guy,” he whispered, leaning in close. “Look, most we knife lovers ain’t cut for robes or songs, but once in a while we all slips and needs a fixin. If yer tans broken, find Mik at the docks and he’ll set it right, but watch the lows next tav run.”  

I backed off and nodded. I flipped him a dime and slipped my hand over my mouth. The gesture said, “All’s quiet.” I found where the leathers were hidden and pulled out the bundle, then carefully made my way to the docks. It wasn’t hard to spot Mik, since he was a fixture of the docks, a beggar man with bright red hair and no left foot. “Oi, Mik,” I said, coming in with my bundle. “Need a bit more heat tonight?”  

Mik knew me, too. I had been living as a bum and cutpurse since I got to town, and meeting the other bums had been the first part of acclimating. I wasn’t too familiar or strange to him. “Oi Bark, always needin a bit more ‘eat anight if’n ya can spare it.”  

“Lots to spare tonight,” I said, looking around. “Dar says ya can fix me if I done slipped a bit.”
