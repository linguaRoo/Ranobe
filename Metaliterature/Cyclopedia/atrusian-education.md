# Education on Atrusius

## Pre-School

A family is generally responsible for the early education of ages 0-3 years old. This includes age-appropriate fluency in the public language of the city for speech and signing, usually including the ability to at least read the most common phonetic form of the languages, and the ability to count and form numbers composed of one or two numeral roots -- in Adinjo this is counting from _mijin_ (zero) to _qimmoqin_ (eleven-dozen-eleven, or one-hundred-forty-three).

It is usually expected that a three-and-a-half year old child (approximately 5 Gaean years old) can do simple addition and socialize well enough to receive an allowance and pay for small purchases (though it may still be expected for an elder family member to be present for such purchases, depending on the specific species and local culture). They are also generally expected to be able to navigate streets during daytime to visit friends or shops within 1 block of their home. They are not generally expected to run errands at this age, but before the fourth birthday, it is common for children to be able to do so if they want something or if the rest of their family is not fit to run the errand.

## Standard Chennur Education

### Chennur’grat (ages 4-5); _urban only_

The earliest formal schooling for most children at a _chennur_ lasts for about three hours for the first year (usually beginning when a child is 4), and focuses on advancing the speaking, signing, literacy, and numeracy of children so they can read, count, and add and subtract more quickly. The primary expectations of first year education include playing well with others, sharing, and a sense of creative expression through simple arts and crafts. The child finishing their first year should be able to do simple addition and subtraction with up to two digits, read an age-appropriate book aloud without stumbling, and produce creative expression in some manner.

In US American terms, this is roughly equivalent to kindergarten and first grade.

### Chennur’tal (ages 5-8); _urban and rural_

The second through fourth years of school build upon the foundations of first-year, with increasing complexity in each subsequent year. From this year, school is typically 3 hours in the morning and 3 hours in the afternoon, with a one-hour break for lunch during which students may often go home for their meal.

#### Second Year (ages 5-6; grades 2-3)

Multiplication, currency conversion, and foundational geometry (generally: shape names and definitions) between _tendi_ and _daimos_ is added to the mathematical curriculum. The language curriculum adds more complex reading and starts to include creative writing. The creative arts tend to allow more diverse media and forms of expression, often including simple poetry. Students begin to learn about the local world and history during second year.

#### Third Year (ages 6-7; grade 4-5)

Division and fractions are added to the curriculum, with the equivalent of decimal notated fractions by the end of the year. Exponentiation is introduced. The language curriculum begins to explore rhetoric and composition through analysis, reproduction, public performance, and original creation of essays or speeches. History tends to expand beyond the local city-state to international events, but remains in broad strokes. Some exploration of historical government systems may occur. The sciences enter the curriculum, with a focus on scientific methodology and foundational earth and life sciences. Most third year students will be expected to compose and perform a speech, propose and perform a simple (usually highly safe and repeatable) science experiment, and to produce a work of art (song, poem, image, etc.) by the end of the year.

#### Fourth Year (ages 7-8; grade 6-7)

Variables, grouping operations, and functions are added to the math curriculum. Early algebraic understanding is developed. Language arts refine development of rhetorical skills in composition and analysis. Book reports exploring the (likely) intentions of authors are expected work. History becomes more detailed and relevant. The essential scientific elements of the physical sciences are taught, primarily through primitive engineering and chemistry studies. Rhetoric develops a focus on insight and synthesis, combining analysis of intention with personal experience and reflection on a text, as well as how to construct instructional and persuasive wording. Journaling becomes a primary focus to develop daily writing habits and skills. History moves to a world scale, but is more in depth. Sciences expand to include low-tier thaumic sciences.

Social skills developed by this period include collaboration and team projects, sometimes leading to the impromptu creation of _dandé_ groups for creative projects. This is generally the last year when a whole cohort will have the same lessons across a full day of school, as it is the last year of the lower school.

### Chennur’dul (ages 8-13); _urban, limitd rural_

Often translated as “academy” this level of school is equivalent to the high school years of US Americans. It begins at age 8 and runs through age 12, and while four of the six hours of schooling are shared, there are two hours per day dedicated to specializing the skills of students in the equivalent of elective classes.

In addition to this, many trades offer apprenticeships to students enrolled in _chennur’dul_, during after-school hours. It is socially expected that students enrolled in such apprenticeships still prioritize their studies until they are 12, and the tasks offered to such apprentices typically start with learning and maintaining the work environment -- cleaning and organizing workshops while be able to observe and learn the basics of the trade.

#### Fifth Year (ages 8-9; grade 7-8)

The common courses in Fifth Year are typically basic algebra and geometry for maths, home economics as a general life skills course, literary history and review for language arts and history, and alchemistry—a combination of mundane and magical chemistry—as the standard sciences course.

Common electives available starting in this year include penmanship (specifically learning calligraphy and foreign scripts), various arts and crafts, second languages, and sports or social skills. Magical studies are typically not included in fifth year electives.

#### Sixth Year (ages 9-10; grade 8-9)

The common courses in Sixth Year are typically advanced algebra and basic trigonometry for maths, biology and common health knowledge as sciences and life skills, foreign literature for language arts and social studies, and physical fitness including basic self defense (which will remain a common course through the remainder of academy).

Common electives available starting in this year include foreign cultural exchange, basic utility magic (the equivalent of cantrips), and soft skills related to future workplaces.

#### Seventh Year (ages 10-11; grade 9-10)

The common courses in Seventh Year are typically economics, physics, philosophy, and physical fitness including basic self defense with weapons.

There are generally no newly available electives during Seventh or Eighth year, allowing students to further advance their knowledge in electives started in the previous years, or broadening their interests by starting to explore other electives.

#### Eighth Year (ages 11-12; grade 10-11)

The common courses in Eighth Year are typically everyday law and contracts to prepare students to read and understand legal paperwork in their adult life, civic government and policies to prepare students to understand how their local government works, etiquette, and physical fitness including basic self defense with magic or weapons.

Electives during the Eighth Year are used to improve one’s studies within prior electives or broaden one’s base of knowledge.

### Chennur’dyur (ages 12-13); _urban only_

#### Ninth Year (ages 12-13; grade 11-12)

The Ninth Year is also called _chennur’dyur_ or capstone. During this year, schools will often provide rich simulations of real adult life rather than traditional subject-focused classes, often using pouches of play coins (often made from seashells, non-tender metals like brass, or mundane wood) to give students the final hands-on experience of managing their life and finances. The Ninth Year common course usually only runs in the morning hours, and students may opt out of afternoon classes to develop their apprenticeship or real-world skills, though they may also remain in school for electives during the afternoon elective periods.

> [!Info] Life Experience  
> A notable example of this life experience can be found in the history of STAR Dandé, who started publishing the first year of their meten series _Rashi’hemlé_ (translates to “To the Stars”) during their ninth year. 

Ninth year students are generally recognized as full legal adults, and the _chennur’dyur_ is widely recognized as the last stage of ensuring that as many young people as possible develop into upstanding adult citizens.

## Apprenticeships (ages 8+)

Apprenticeships are a secondary form of education which may or not be paid when the apprentice is still a student. In these arrangements, a master accepts the labor of the apprentice in exchange for lessons on how to perform the work necessary in the trade, craft, or profession. The master is expected to also provide sufficient material and time during the years of apprenticeship to enable the apprentice to develop their skills. Apprenticeships typically last a minimum of six years, so starting an apprenticeship while still in academy gives young adults a head start over those who put off their apprenticeships until after graduation.

Apprentices are generally grouped into six levels: Student, Novice, Beginner, Peer, Laborer, and Traveler. Each rank typically sees a higher level of compensation, and expected labor, than the previous. A Traveler is free to leave their Master’s business and start their own, though many traveler apprentices take on leadership roles within their master’s workshop or business until they achieve Expert or Master certification from the guild (the other ranks are all assigned by the Master). Technically, the master of a workshop does not _need_ to be a guild certified Master: Travelers, Experts, and Masters are all able to take on apprentices.

## Achennur (ages 13+)

Some professions require more dedicated study than the nine years of mandatory _chennur_, and so the _achennur_ exists for those who want to dedicate themselves to knowledge, research, and innovation in the sciences (natural and magical), philosophy, and certain arts. The study period in _achennur_ is generally 4-6 years, and may be followed by becoming educational assistants, dedicated researchers or engineers, or archivists, among other career options. These positions often involve salaries every 14 or 44 days depending on seniority.
