# A Day as a Wage Labourer

> [!info] Biography
> 
> This is a day in the life of Darkam, an 16 year old Ork (roughly 24 Gaean years old) living in Kamelon. He lives on the port level, in a decent neighborhood that receives natural light mostly in the later part of the day, and is a regular guest at his local tavern, the Blackiron Anchor Pub. He is currently courting an Ork woman named Erkama who lives a neighborhood over and works at a laundry and clothing mending facility that focuses its services on dockworkers and visiting sailors, people who are often in great and regular need of these services.
> 
> Darkam works as a stocker and clerk at a fresh market near the port, including regular trips to the harbor for fresh seafood, and he works the krestu shift. He is on very good terms with his employer, a middle-aged Ork named Lang who has a glass left eye due to an adventuring incident in his own youth.

# The Morning Bell

It was a rough night. I was out drinking until almost 22:00, and needed to pay a local kid to help me stumble home. I hope I didn’t tip him too generously, all I remember is giving him two daimos, but not which faces they had. The morning bell is ringing to signal the end of dark shift, so it’s the first watch already. Still, if I close my eyes again, I think I’ll sleep through at least a whole watch, and I hate being late.

I work at the Sailor’s Stock freshmarket. I’m on track to be a shift manager, but right now I still mostly handle stocks and deliveries. Well, pick-ups really. After lunch, I run to the harbor for the morning fishers’ hauls, cart ‘em back, and stock up the afternoon fish bins. Yeah, I’m actually gettin’ up. I definitely drank too much. If I were topside, the dawn would be splitting my head, but it never gets bright in the mornings down here.

I stumble into the toilet and do what I need to. At least we have running taps to wash our hands. “Keep yer hands clean in this work,” the old grocer never lets us forget it. Don’t get me wrong, old man Lang is a great boss, a lot better than my old manager back up topside, but he’s a tough bastard and repeats himself more than I need him to. Guess some of the younger kids don’t listen as good as me, though.

Clean hands. I step into the pantry and pull out a few roots, some eggs, and a bit of sea bacon. Just a simple hash for breakfast. Lots of pepper on top, and a splash of oralen hot sauce after cooking to make a proper hangover breakfast. I wash up the dishes and leave them on a wooden rack to dry. It took a while to get used to actually washing up right away, but losing a pot to a rust slime learned me that lesson quick. I grab my daily book, turn up the light crystal, and review my notes for today. It’s Fireday today, planning a family dinner with ma and pa, topside. I need to check if Erkama can make it. And I’m supposed to pick up a big load of seafood. Maybe big enough I’ll need a hand. But most of the afternoon is open after I get the seafood all stocked up and collect my pay.

I stop by a newssheet stand on my way into work. I pick up the daily and the new _Delver’s Collection_. Freshly stocked, and I can read a bit before I start working. If you don’t know, _DC_ is a combination anthology, literary and meten, and I’ve been hooked on this literary delve series for a few years: _Howls in the Darkness_. It focuses on wilderness dungeons, y’know the ones that ain’t underground. The main reason I don’t wanna set foot out of a wall without a damn good reason. But the news comes first.

My neighborhood cross team is doing well in the city league. Might even be worth dropping a bit on them at the tav. Clear weather on the surface. Warning for the dark shift weather, storms starting around 8th watch, high tides and waves might even slow the port down overnight. I see a familiar beggar wandering around the park and toss her a sunbloom coin. I know it ain’t much, but I also know she tells good stories once she’s settled in for the day. She smiles and offers to tell me a tale, but I tell her to just enjoy it, and I might bring her something to eat after work.

The rest of the walk to the freshmarket is uneventful. I’m about ten blocks away, so it takes a while to make the walk, but I can smell the bakers and the breakfast carts. If I hadn’t already eaten, I might pick up something. Despite my reason, I do end up picking up some fried pastries at one of the pastry shops. One for myself with an earthy sugar dusting for myself, half a dozen to share with team. One daimos for the lot. But I’m always more generous on payday.

I walk into the front door of Lang’s place and shout my usual greetings. He’s been open with Ralien the Kenyu and Mordon the Peratan for a while now, so I get my usual responses. Mordon perks up when he smells the pastries. “Team treats from Darkam? Now I’m sure it’s payday!” I nod and chuckle. It’s almost a routine on Firedays, and usually Lightningday too. “I made sure to get you a redberry jam,” I say. “Rali, they didn’t have the isa cream ready yet. Want me to spot you for yours on lunch?” She politely declines the offer. I still have a bit before I start my shift, and Lang hates overtime. Says it cuts his margins too thin. So I set up in the lounge room next to his office and read a bit of my story.

## Second Watch

The bell for second watch is about to sound. I make sure to tie my apron tight, and wash my hands again. Lang pokes his head in the lounge and wishes me a good shift. He reminds me about the size of the seafood order today, and asks if I need any help. I nod. “Yeah, I might want to take one of the stronger team members with me,” I say. “Karosh maybe. If he’s not too busy on maintenance.” Lang nods back. “S’long as nothin’ pops up I think we can spare him for the pickup.”

I started my work, stocking the produce and helping customers find what they needed. We actually deal with more sailors than locals most days, so it’s common to get folks looking for out-of-season seafood and produce, and we usually don’t get incoming products for a day or two after any given ship is unloaded, just for guild processing times. Once in a while, I have to step in if a few sailors get too rowdy with the rest of the team. I had to do that just before lunch today.

## Third Watch

A few Kenyu sailors were pressing Mordon about why we didn’t have any squid in stock when we had it last time they were here. They got a bit close, and I came up behind Mordon. “Local squid are less active this time of year,” I explained. “They go deeper than the fisher’s nets. When’s the last time ya was in?”

The sailor with the fairest hair and most tattoos looked me over. “We seen lots of squids on the way in!” One of his friends, with darker hair in tight curls, grabbed his shoulder and said. “It’s been half a year, Dal,” he said. He tried to settle his friend down. “Sorry fer the trouble.” But Dal wasn’t ready to let up. “We got squid here last time we was in town, Falas. I wan’ me another squid!”

I’m bigger than these sailors, but I ain’t really a fighter. Mordon backed behind me. “We got a shipment comin’ this afternoon,” I said taking a deep breath and squaring my shoulders. It wasn’t unheard of for a fishing ship to get some squid even out of season. Maybe I could work this to my advantage. “The fishers ain’t in for the day yet. So when I go to pick up the shipment, I’ll check if anybody gots some squid in their nets. And I’ll guarantee you that if there’s any squid in today, ya get the first pick this afternoon.”

That seemed to do the trick. “It’s just too early? Fine!” Dal pulled a small purse off his belt and counted a few daimos coins into it. “I’m about to trust you to get me some squid, but I expect you to find it no matter what. This purse is your bonus when you deliver.”

I nodded. Sailors could be generous. I hadn’t paid close attention to the coins, but they were all silver. At least an hour’s wage for a tip. But we didn’t really do special orders. “If it exists, I’ll get it for ya. But our stock is for all of our customers, no special thanks necessary.”

Situation defused. Dal and his crew headed out, promising to be back in the afternoon. As I headed to lunch, Lang waved me over. “Just a mo,” he said. “Mordon tol’ me bout that crew. Y’know we pro’ly ain’t gettin’ no squid, right?”

“I know it ain’t regular shipment,” I said. “Was gonna ask ya if we could check the other sources. If Karosh handles the main cart, I can check with some of the other fishers while the main shipment gets loaded. I know it’s a cost, but we could charge a premium for it today if we get any.”

Lang squinted, looking up at the ceiling. “Hmm. I’ll tink it over. See me after lunch, maybe I give ya a purse to cover it.”

### Lunch Break

Our lounge used to be Lang’s common room before he opened the market. He still keeps his original dining table in the middle, with a stoveplate stored in the center if anybody wants to cook. Most days, nobody does, but Karosh, a particularly tall Semeric, was in early and frying up a minced beef and lamb blend with strong aromatic herbs, and pointed to some flatbreads. “Jalban for everybody,” he said. “Me’s brother bought up a load of meat durin’ ‘is stay, and now me’s gotta go t’rough it by t’morra. Figured sharin’ with yous’all is a good use o’ ‘is graces.”

I nodded and pulled out a nice, thick cushion to sit on. Graushan, a lean Gramurn, came in a moment later, walking to the lounge washbasin and cleaning his hands. He grabbed a couple of cushions and pulled out a box of sliced raumesh and cheese curds. He offered me a bit, and I graciously accepted. The deep red fuit was a great teaser to lunch, and the rich savory and slightly acidic flavor went well with the fresh white semi-bitter cheese curds. Karosh set aside a wooden plate and asked Graushan for a bit. I hadn’t brought anything myself except the morning pastries.
