# Atrusian Publishing

Atrusian publishing is a primary economic industry which is regulated in part by only being able to own physical property. Because they are not able to own intellectual property, they must enter licensing contracts with authors and artists to have anything novel to publish, or rely entirely on works which are in the public domain, where they must compete for either the lowest price or highest quality (or the best ration between price and quality).

Publishers own presses and materials, with the most wealthy publishing houses producing most of their own paper and at least their basic inks, and have distribution networks to help creators get their works out to wider audiences.

## Publishing Standards

The Folio and Anthology magazine are typically printed on pages roughly equivalent to Gaean A3 pages folded in half to produce 4 pages per sheet and bound together in sequence with thread. Both generally have very similar glossy, full-colour covers, while the quality of their interior pages differs by format.

Later in the life of a story, chapters will be bound together in compiled novel formats -- hard-bound versions will typically retain the original size (sometimes using A4-sized pages which aren’t folded), while soft-bound versions are often produced at half-scale -- roughly A4-sized pages assembled in the same manner. These compilations are usually between 240-480 pages, and sometimes the left-right layout of the pages may change slightly in the compilation layout process, particularly if there are no spreads that need to be presented in their original layout. In the case of compiled folios, the bonus contents and the main story are sometimes laid out so that the main stories all take the “main stage” of the book with bonus contents all moved to the end of the book.

Serialized literary novels are usually edited every 37-38 chapters into individual novels, so authors of such works are encouraged to pace their stories to provide good ending points around these lengths, while serialized illustrated novels tend to end every single chapter in strong but open ways.

### Meten

Meten are fully illustrated stories, equivalent to Gaean comic books, manga, manhua, etc. They range from small _luxamet_ or four-panel formats to fully developed narratives in almost every genre. They may be published in newssheets, folios, or anthology magazines (which may be meten-only or have mixed formats), and some even start publication as periodic graphic novels between 120-240 pages long (as opposed to graphic novel compilations discussed above). 

### Qontan

Qontan are fully textual stories, equivalent to Gaean literature of various genres and eras. Qontan may sometimes have supplementary illustrations, but do not require them, and when they do include them, the illustrations may change between major re-releases.

### Meqontan

Meqontan are fully integrated illustrated stories. While many of them are made for young children, there is not actually a strict age range for the style of composition. Many more mature oriented meqontan use advanced typesetting to wrap around the images in way that accent both the text and imagery. Meqontan are almost always published as whole product books, not in serialized publications.

## Publication Frequency

Most anthology magazines publish on 7-day or 14-day cycles, releasing either the same day of every short week or the same day of every long week. Within these magazines, stories are generally published in either every publication or every other publication, though alternate issue frequency is far less common in the fortnightly magazines due to their already slower pace. The primary cover stories of these anthologies are 12-24 pages long, and anthology magazines typically run a minimum of 4 new and ongoing series while often running at least 1 reprinted series.
