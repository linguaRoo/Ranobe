# Atrusian Architecture - A Primer

Architecture on Atrusius is heavily influenced by the residents and local culture of the cities where it exists, but there are some broad general principles available.

> [!info] Ideals, Averages, and Maximums
> 
> This document uses ideal circumstances and measurements which reflect maximum standards. On average, many spaces conform to areas which do not follow a perfect square grid and need to be adjusted. While modern, cosmopolitan extensions of cities may adhere more closely to these measurements and shapes, Atrusian cultures tend to work _with_ natural landforms and natural paths. Older cities, towns, and even modern unwalled villages tend to have more organic paths and layouts than implied by the idealized numbers laid out here.

## Green Space

Surface cities value the presence of green space that serves the public interest, from public parks to local greenyards. In general, a generous communal courtyard (often called a _greenyard_ regardless of its specific features) is set in the middle of every residential block, with hallways or alleyways to access it without needing to go through residences. In some neighborhoods, yards of different types may be offered to better suit the preferences of the block.

## City Blocks

A city block is roughly one square furlong—about 660 rods squared—as permitted by the geography of the city. Individual housing lots are usually about 66 rods wide by 132 rods deep, with the remaining interior space reserved for a communal courtyard.

> [!info] Ideal
> 
> The measurement of an individual housing lot is probably the most consistent standard when taken as a whole area: 66 rods by 132 rods gives an area of about 0.2 acres. This is the standard urban size of a single housing lot, and even in imperfectly, irregularly, or non-square shaped blocks, the 0.2 acre measurement is preserved to the best of the geographical abilities of the city planners.

City blocks are often a combination of public and personal housing, usually no more than 50% of the lots are allowed for public housing, though such housing is usually built with denser packing and more levels than personal housing. As a general rule, no more than 20% of the housing lots on a block may be used for private rentable housing, though consolidating adjacent lots can make this number seem more flexible.

## Neighborhoods

A neighborhood covers the streets of an area covering roughly 12-16 city blocks. No more than 50% of the lots in a neighborhoos are typically allocated for public housing, and usually only 20% of a neighborhood is allocated for commercial housing. It is common for at least one block in a neighborhood to be reserved as a park, most often one of the central blocks, though sometimes larger parks may be made to serve several neighborhoods.

As a general rule, there is no cap on how many plots across a neighborhood may be used for commercial purposes other than commercial housing (rentals, taverns, inns, hotels, etc.)—residents who own personal property are widely encouraged to work near or even from their home, provided that their work does not cause a general health hazard to the community. Because of this, many personal housing lots have homes designed in such a way to serve as a workshop, clinic, restaurant, or other small businesses with the residential space upstairs.

## Civic Infrastructure

In general, there will be a _chennur_, or school building, in every neighborhood to cover education starting around 4 years old and ending at 7 or 9 years old, roughly covering US grades 1 to either 6 or 8. Every few neighborhoods will share a _chennur’dul_, a “greater school” often translated as “academy,” that serves like a US high school, to cover education from either 8 or 10 to graduation year at age 12. There will often be some amount of overlap between some of the common chennur and the chennur’dul, allowing some flexibility of transition from the local neighborhood school to the broader community academy. While rare, the biggest cities may have a dedicated split between “junior” and “senior” academies, somewhat akin to US junior and senior high schools.

## Species-Based Architecture

While this topic will eventually be explored more thoroughly in documents per species, some overviews will be provided here.

### Adin Architecture

Adin architecture is usually slightly elevated from the ground level (the ground floor level is usually elevated by 1-2 rods), and usually has a sheltered patio, porch, or pergola in the rear. If used entirely for residence, the front door will usually open to a small vestibule with a coat and shoe closet with a large open main room that serves as the equivalent to a modern living room, dining room, and guest bedroom. The back of the house will include a kitchen, pantry or larder, a toilet room, and for single-story houses, the bedrooms.

For two-story houses used exclusively as residences, bedrooms will tend to be upstairs, with the stairs located in the entry vestibule, most often opposite the coat closet. The toilet room may also be extended or adjacent to a bathing room. Families larger than 4 members usually have at least two stories on their house.

Cellars or basements are not uncommon, usually accessed from the back yard or a stairway from the vestibule. When they are included, the elevation of the ground floor is used to ensure space for windows in the basement walls. Basements are often used for root cellars, home fermentation, and storage of seasonal items (such as winter clothing or holiday decorations) while they are out of season.

Adin architecture tends to favor straight lines and strong angles, usually right angles or slight hexagonal protrusions (akin to bay windows), and gently angled roofs with strong overhangs to cast cooling shade during midday.

If the family operates any businesses from the home, the main room of a single-story home will also be the meeting room for the business, while two-story houses will dedicate most of the ground floor to public business uses (often including extra-large windows on the ground floor to distinguish that they are a business). They may still have a private vestibule for coats and shoes on the ground floor, with the stairs located in the private vestibule. 

### Gramurn Architecture

Gramurn architecture is often built with its ground floor elevated by around 3 rods, and almost always includes cellar space which can be accessed from at least two points in the house -- usually one interior and one exterior. Gramurn prefer to use skypaper for windows in personal areas as it diffuses visible light rather than providing a full view outside. Gramurn architecture tends to have more gentle staircases than other species, with more spacious vestibules which may include washbasins for shoes and feet.

Gramurn living spaces tend to be very open, and they often sleep communally on large futon-like matttresses. This sleeping arrangement allows Gramurn families to comfortably be larger than the individual bedding arrangements most other species prefer. The main room of a gramurn house usually opens directly to the kitchen, which usually has a pot on low heat throughout the day and night. Gramurn generally prefer public bathing to private, and so rarely build a bathing room.

Gramurn architecture prefers gently curved shapes and high ceilings -- the main room of a gramurn house may have a balcony “hallway” for the second floor allowing a single high window to light up most of the house, with circular or elliptical windows in bedrooms that still allow a lot of diffused light in.

One particular quirk of Gramurn architecture is the den structure, which can involve covering the main structures of a house in large amounts of dirt which simulate the burrowing habits of their historical cultures and also provide strong thermal insulation. In urban areas, dens are usually limited to two stories with dome-like roofing to support the weight of the earthworks, though outside of walled cities, there are known to be elaborate three-story dens in some farming villages. Dens usually utilize long tunnel-windows or organically-styled architecture to ensure that light can reach the interior while still feeling like a burrow in a hill.

### Hol’tiin Architecture

Unlike most other species, the Hol’tiin prefer to have a foundation about 1-2 rods below the local ground level, providing a feeling of shelter and safety to their reptilian brains by being lower. They tend to make stone floors and use heated water pipes or magic to keep their homes at around 20 degrees Celsius. Unlike mammalian species, they do not grow uncomfortable or lazy even in summer heat, and may use their slightly-sunken floors (disabling the heating!) to offer coolhouses to neighbors in the hottest months.

Hol’tiin houses are often built with more openings for airflow, and unlike most species, they are more likely to use a second story for public business than personal privacy. Hol’tiin build for control of heat and humidity in their living spaces on the ground floor, while making sure that other floors (if any) are not distressed by their comfort needs. They tend to use stone, clay, bricks, and concretes, with skypaper windows that provide diffuse light.
