# Time on Atrusius

Atrusius is a planet approximately 110 light-years from Gaea in a mana-rich planetary system. It is larger than Gaea and orbits at a greater distance from its star, Adtresk, as the fourth planet within its system of seven planets.

Atrusius orbits Adtresk in about 528.33 days, and its rotational speed produces days which are are within a negligible approximation of Gaean days. For all practical purposes: days, hours, minutes, and seconds on Atrusius are identical to those same measurements on Gaea or in our real world.

That said, there are other measurements of time which differ. As noted, the year of Atrusius is 528.33 days, or 528 days with a leap year roughly every third year. The current epoch of Atrusius begins in AA 0, with AA 2 being the initial leap year under the current calendar. Every 30th year is skipped from this leap year cycle, but every 150th year remains included. The current year (as of CE 2026-06-13 in the real world) is AA 373.

## Weeks

The standard Adin week is a 14-day cycle named after elements and ideals. This calendar began on Gaea, with each such week roughly aligned with the cycle of the Gaean moon from New to Full and Full back to New. This is the fortnightly week sometimes called the _larnchanbia_ or “two-seven-day,” while the basic _chanbia_ is equivalent to a normal Gaean week -- except that the Adin name all fourteen days, and so treat the fortnight as the primary weekly cycle.

There are different variant weeks among some of the other cultures of Atrusius, the Adin week is the version used most consistently in the documents. Weeks flow seamlessly between months much like they do on modern Gaea: if a month begins on a Kuhajia, it will end on the fourth Luanjia, with the next month beginning on a Romjia.

Note that fluent Adinjo speakers do not think of these days by the English equivalents: the English equivalents are provided specifically for convenient understanding for our English audience.

## Months

The Atrusian year is typically divided into an even 12 months of 44 days each. The leap day is applied at the end of the year, and may be treated as the 45th day of the 12th month, the 1st (and only) day of a 13th pseudo-month, or simply as an annual leap day between the two years. Regardless of how it is denoted, it is placed after the 44th day of Month 12. There are two primary international naming conventions for the months, though much of our current documentation uses a third Localized convention that is not widely used on Atrusius.

The length of a month is just a few days more than same period of time it takes for Gurion—the largest and slowest of the three moons—to complete a full lunar phase cycle, which is around 41.86 days. The standard 14-day week lines up closely with the 16.11-day period of the Hyfel, the half-cycle of Gerjui which takes about 29.28 days, and the full cycle of Gurion. Traditional calendars may use these lunar cycles in various ways and are not explored in this document at present.

#### Banking Months

There have at some points in the development of Atrusius been thoughts of including a banking month with a period of 2 fortnights. We are noting the possibility of banking months, but are neither confirming nor denying their existence at this point in time. Because the current standard for banking relies specifically on fortnightly payment cycles, we are uncertain if having a banking-specific or banking-centered month would serve as appropriate or needless complication.

## Seasons

Due to various factors, Atrusius generally experiences about 6 seasons, which are roughly 88 days long. For practical purposes, these seasons are generally said to be exactly two months long, though certain festivals are planned around the actual astronomical alignments associated with the changing or midpoints of different seasons.

The seasons are inverted across the northern and southern hemispheres much like earth. The cycle of seasons across a year is (as translated into English) Wet Winter, Spring, Wet Summer, Dry Summer, Autumn, Dry Winter. The specific conditions associated with each season vary wildly by the geography of specific areas, but the wet seasons tend to be more prone to mundane storms while the dry seasons are more prone to manaïc “weather” conditions -- manastorms and dungeon floods most notably.

Despite their name, the _dry_ seasons are only relatively drier than the wet seasons, they are not inherently devoid of humidity or precipitation (though this can vary by the geography of the locale). In this case, the _dry_ refers to the way the regular shifting of the mana field makes mana more volatile during these months, similar to how dry weather can make static electricity more reactive.

## Daily Time

While the daily period of Atrusius is equivalent to the daily period of Gaea, local time can be measured in several ways. The most common local system is the system of watches (_klìk_, pl. _klìku_ or _clicks_), which are 2-hour long blocks divided into either 120 or 144 minutes. While the standard documentation uses 120 minutes that are equal to Gaean minutes, the Adinjo watch system uses a 50-second _tok_ with 72 _toku_ to the short _klìk_ (an hour long) and 144 minutes to the standard or long _klìk._ The measurement of this clock treats 06:00 in the Gaean notation as 00:00 within its own notation.

While we will note the existence of this system, we currently intend to continue documenting time using a Gaean 24-hour clock notation, with the general use of hours, minutes, and seconds. This is to minimize the confusion of our audience and asking them to shift the clock by six hours or deal with double-hours.

### Shifts

While individual work shifts for wage laborers on Atrusius may begin at hour, they usually begin specifically at even-numbered hours and end seven hours later (for full-time positions). There are four broad shifts, _krestu_, _jurabi_, _kolmen_, and _coci_, which cover the span of a day, and ctually cover a 28-hour window due to the seven-hour standard length of shifts including their lunch breaks.

The standard day shifts are _krestu_ (morning) and _jurabi_ (midday or afternoon). _Krestu_ shift begins from 05:00 to 11:00, ending from 12:00 to 18:00. _Jurabi_ shifts begin from 11:00 to 17:00, ending from 18:00 to 00:00.

The Dark shifts include _kolmen_ and _coci_ shifts. _Kolmen_ shifts start from 17:00 to 23:00, ending from 00:00 to 07:00. _Kolmen_ shifts in certain industries, or extending beyond 00:00, may be classified as part of the _Dark Shift_ and include a premium—usually 1/3 of the base pay (2,0 to 3,44 daimos)—for hazardous positions like dock or warehouse work, night guards, and sanitation maintenance. _Coci_ shifts start from 20:00 to 03:00, ending from 03:00 to 10:00. All _coci_ shifts are classified as part of the dark shift, including premium compensation.

## Relative Time

### Growing Up & Perceived Time

Te perception of time is largely based on how much of one’s life one has experienced. Because all species develop from birth to physiological maturity at about the same rate as jaldin humans, this means children of all species perceive time roughly equally, and peers tend to have a similar physiochemical neural experience of time and its passage as their lives go on. While there is some cultural influence that helps prime longer-lived species, it remains difficult to truly understand the scale of a zenade out of a two-grossury life before one completes at least one grossury. This means that younger members of every species live more similarly to their peers than the elders of their species.

The first large shift in many of the mid-to-long-lived species happens around 5 zenades of age, when their Orkish peers start to die from accrued injuries or showing the signs of old age. By the end of their first grossury, they usually step back from intense labor in favor of producing higher quality works or products, or even moving from physical to intellectual efforts. Interestingly, some individuals within the longer-lived species will actually do the opposite, finding actual public service more important for some time late in their first grossury, or early in the second, as they honor the lives of their shorter-lived childhood friends. It’s surprisingly common for Kenyu and Semeric in particular who had no prior interest in military service to enlist in domestic or international military service between 120 and 160 years old.

By the middle of their second grossury (around 216 years old) the weight of long life often starts to exert itself. Peratan, Hol’tiin, and Gramurn peers having passed away, Adin begin to experience their aging, Semeric professionals dedicate, or rededicate, themselves to their life’s passion craft, and Kenyu begin to recognize that the world around them isn’t really their world any longer. By the end of a second grossury, many Kenyu find themselves withdrawing from cosmopolitan societies as their homes, and returning to Kenyu-centric nations and societies that can understand the weight of long life, and support them in the passions they do still have.
