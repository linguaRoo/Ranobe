# Adinjo Numerics

The Adin numeric system is fundamentally base-twelve. They have unique numeral words for values from zero to twelve, and standard words for powers of twelve.

- **mijin:** zero
- **win:** one
- **larn:** two
- **jon:** three
- **luxan:** four
- **kom:** five
- **lum:** six
- **chan:** seven
- **kosij:** eight
- **mu:** nine
- **shak:** ten
- **qin:** eleven
- **tomo:** twelve

Larger numbers are made by compounding:

- **tomowin:** thirteen (lit. “twelve-one”)
- **tomolarn:** fourteen (lit. “twelve-two”)
- **tomomu:** twenty-one (lit. “twelve-nine”)
- **lammo:** (sometimes _larmo_ or _larmmo_) twenty-four (from _larn+mo_; literally “two-twelve”)
- **jommo:** thirty-six (literally “three-twelve”)
- **luxammo:** forty-eight
- **chammo:** eighty-four (literally “seven-twelve”)
- **kosimmo:** (sometimes _kosimo_ or _kosmo_) ninety-six (literally “eight-twelve”)
- **shakmo:** (sometimes _shamo_) one-hundred-twenty (literally “ten-twelve”)
- **qimmo:** one-hundred-thirty-two (literally “eleven-twelve”)

And the larger powers of twelve currently documented are:

- **qori:** gross, one-hundred-forty-four; often shortened to _-ri_ as a counting suffix following similar structures to those used by multiples of _tomo_
- **tóji:** great-gross, one-thousand seven-hundred-twenty-eight; often shortened to _-ji_ or _-óji_ as a counting suffix following similar structures to those used by multiples of _tomo_
- **kími:** grand-gross, twenty-thousand seven-hundred-thirty-six; usually retains its full form in compound numbers, may be suffixed to the full forms or _tomo_, _qori_, and _tóji_ to compose the next three powers of twelve (the 5th, 6th, and 7th powers); culturally equivalent to “ten thousand”

Generally for the initial counts of the powers, you do not need to use _win_ to establish “one” _tomo_ and the like, however you must use it in compound numbers of larger scale: _qori-wimmo_ for a value of 156, of “gross and one dozen.”

- **tomo-kími:** a dozen kími, 248,832 (base-10); culturally equivalent to “hundred thousand”
- **qori-kími:** a gross kími, 2,985,984 (base-10); culturally equivalent to “million”
- **tóji-kimi:** a great-gross kími, 35,831,808 (base-10); culturally equivalent to “ten million”
- **kikími:** a kími kími, 429,981,696 (base-10); culturally equivalent to “hundred million” or “countless, innumerable”

> [!info] Undocumented Numbers  
> There is currently no documentation on numbers beyond _kikími_, so it is unclear to us if the next three powers follow the same pattern before reaching a new base word, or if the next power after _kikími_ is a new base.

## Fractions

- **enla:** half, demi-, semi-, partial; (broad) formal but not natural relationship
- **i:** of, related to, from
- **da’:** point, dot, mark; the reading of the fractional marker for dozenal notation, akin to English “point” in reading a decimal number

Fractional numbers can be composed primarily by adding _i-_ as a prefix to the denominator. In some cases it will be a connector between the numerator and denominator. If reading a dozenal fraction such as 1,2 the readng would be _win hi larni_ “one and two-i,” adding the _-i_ suffix at the end of the read number, or _win (hi) larn tomo’i_ “one (and) two twelve-i,”indicating the specific fractional depth. The casual preference is for the former reading.

However, there is the specific case of _enla_: this word is refers to half-things, like _chanjia-enla_ would hypothetically be a “half-week.” _Enla_ generally is not used for the numerical value of one-half, but the quantitative measurement of half of something. In this way, it serves the function of English prefixes like half-, semi-, or demito indicate something that is half, or notably, composed of something and some (not-always-specified) other thing without being entirely the named thing.

Unlike most number modifiers, _-enla_ is a suffix. In broader usage, _enla_ also has the connotation of a half-, step-, adopted-, or in-law family member. The specific English connotations of these terms may be extracted with sufficient context, but they are not linguistically distinguished within Adin culture on Atrusius.
