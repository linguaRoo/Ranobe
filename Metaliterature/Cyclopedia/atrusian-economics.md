# The Atrusian Economy

> [!Info] Information  
> The Atrusian economy is not uniform across the world, even though the coins of international trade are standardized and accepted in all Pact-Affiliated nations.
> 
> The notation for fractional elements in Atrusian currency uses a comma as the separator for currency types and a period for every 3 digits of magnitude, such that 1.728,36 is 1728 coins of the primary currency and 36 coins of the next currency down. If combining more currencies or combining non-adjacent currency tiers, the letters x, t, d, g, and p are appended to the end of the number. We will do our best to use the letters for clarity, but may still use the more casual notation at times.

## Coinage

There are five tiers of coins widely used on Atrusius for international trade.

### Mithral Xan

The Mithral Xan is the smallest denomination of coin, and is used primarily in small, local agreements. Xan coins are produced in 1, 6, 12, and 36 coin denominations, and 144 xan are equal to 1 copper tendi.

Xan coins are made by magically fusing scraps of mithral waste from lumber production, essentially filings, shavings, and sawdust, which are valued as a form of tax payment by most governments. The specific magitechnology used to produce these coins is a state secret of the Adin Imperial Government, licensed out to other nations to make the most use out of worldwide mithral resources.

Xan coins are circular and range from about 12-20 mm in diameter based on their denomination. They have a lightly textured side, and they primarily depict a six-pointed starburst on the obverse and their denominated value on the face. A modern Gaean might equate them to having the texture of poker chips.

Xan coins are typically used to pay the finest grades of taxes, and when calculating interest in banking, all currency is converted to xan. If necessary to convert to Gaean currency, it is about USD 0.00025 -- 1/40th of a USD cent.

### Copper Tendi

The copper _tendi_ is the common International coin of the everyday. It is made of copper and pressed in the six standard denominations: 1, 3, 6, 12, 36, and 72 pieces, with increasing mass and size equivalent to the value. 144 tendi are equal to 1 silver daimos.

Tendi are equilateral triangular coins, usually about 18-24 mm to a side, and depicting flowers on the face, and the notation “1t” along with a particular national mint’s seal on the obverse. A 1-tendi coin depicts a generic flower bud on the face, the 3-tendi coin depict a triluné flower (a three-petaled tree flower), the 6-tendi coin depicts a five-petaled flower similar to the Gaean cherry blossom, 12-tendi depicts the Atrusian equivalent to a sunflower, 36-tendi depicts a Gaean water lotus, and 72-tendi depicts the leyroot flower. The individual coins may be referred to by the various local names for the flowers depicted on them.

For simple mathematic conversion, 1 tendi may be broadly treated as the socioeconomic equivalent of USD 0.036 (as of 22 June 2026). Most average people do not regularly use denominations smaller than this except when calculating taxes.

### Silver Daimos

The silver daimos is the practical standard basis of Atrusian currency -- over the course of a week, month, or year, most people think of their income and prices in terms of Daimos. As a general rule of thumb, 1 daimos will purchase 1-2 loaves of fresh bread along with some form of oil, spread, or cheese to accompany it. Daimos are produced in the same standard values as the tendi: 1, 3, 6, 12, 36, and 72 pieces. 144 daimos holds the value of 1 gìlden.

Daimos coins are quadrilateral lozenge shaped, and usually about 18-28mm along their long axis (by denomination) and about 12-18.5mm along the short axis. The faces depict animal heads in profile, while the obverse depicts a pair of pillars and the denominated value of the coin. Daimos coins are often referred to by the names of the animals depicted on them.

For simple mathematical conversion, 1 daimos may be treated as roughly USD 5.00 (as of 22 June 2026). Most wage-labor jobs on Atrusius pay 6-10 daimos (USD 15-25) per hour, with an added 2 hour premium for working a shift of longer than 6 hours (so an 8-hour shift would pay as if it were 10-hours). Salary jobs often pay the equivalent of 10+ daimos per hour. For more on compensation, see the Economics section further down.

### Gold Gìlden

The gold _gìlden_ is the standard currency of business and large commerce. While almost everybody who works earns several gìlden a month, gìlden prices are typically for major investments. Gìldèn are produced in denominations of 1/2, 1, 6, 24, 72, 144, and 432, with mass and size equivalent (not equal) to their value. 1728 gìldèn holds the value of 1 plati.

The gìlden is a rounded lozenge shape, about 24mm wide and 30-42mm tall (by denomination). It depicts symbols of authority (crown, scepter, throne, etc.) on the face along with it denominated value. The obverse is usually scored with mild lines evenly spaced, about 5-6mm apart along the height. These coins may be referred to by the name of the symbol on their face.

For simple mathematical conversion, 1 gìldèn may be treated as roughly USD 720 (as of 26 May 2026). A low-income wage-laborer who works a standard 30-hour week will earn about 7 and 1/2 gìldèn per month, and can live comfortably with some small luxuries and savings. The cost of public housing (publicly-owned rentals, maintained through taxation) for this period is usually around 1 1/2 gìldèn while private housing (personally owned or private rentals) averages around 2 1/2 gìldèn.

### Plati

The Plati is high-value currency that is not suitable for personal use. It is is a large coin, about 12 inches in diameter and 5 kg in mass, that serves as a hold of physical value. There are no denominations of plati—every plati is worth 1728 gìldèn. This coin is primarily used for inter-business and international purposes, and is often considered more of a showpiece and symbol of value than a practical financial tool. Plati are illiquid wealth, and when held by individuals or families, they serve as an anchor of wealth that they cannot spend without breaking it into smaller denominations.

For a simple mathematical conversion, 1 plati may be treated as USD 1,244,160, or much more roughly as USD 1,000,000. Hypothetically, a low-income laborer with no expenses or spendings (entirely supported by parents, for example) would take about 18 years of full-time work to earn 1 plati. If they spend nothing but the minimum for a public one-room house and cost of living, it would take about 432 months, or 36 years, to save the value of 1 plati.

If the value of about 3p 864g (6.230g) were invested in low-but-consistent interest-bearing formats—as low as 1.5% APY or about 312 xan per daimos—the annual interest would be enough to live off at the same standard as a minimum-wage worker, about 93g 108d per year. If held in the form of 3 plati, however, this wealth is absolutely idle in the hands of the holder.

Plati are held primarily by governments and industries as a reserve currency and anchor. They can only be held and displayed or liquidated back into gìldèn.

## Economics

> [!Info] Standard Urban Living  
> The following information assumes fairly standard urban lifestyles on Atrusius and attempts to simplify the entire world rather than being localized to any particular place or time. This should be used purely as a statistical average or ideal, not the specific economics of a particular city.

### Standard Labor Periods

A typical “day” of sold labor is 6 hours, typically with a one hour break for lunch around the middle of a shift (this lunch break is not required to be paid, and if paid is considered a bonus). Wage and salary positions receive premium pay rates for longer working hours, while commission and production positions deal with extended hours based on the labor contract at hand. There is also a class of labor called “free labor” which does not include an explicit contract, but usually pays hourly (almost always at the standard minimum, 6 daimos per hour) with added gratuities or performance-based bonuses on top of the hourly wage.

If divided into shifts, the typical labor shifts may start on roughly any 2-hour mark starting at 6:00 and run for 6-7 hours. Most businesses are open for only about 2 shifts, which may allow for up to 14 hours of full-time workers being present, with non-wage laborers (such as the owner of a business or shift managers) filling out demand during lunch breaks. 

There is an internationally-mandated standard of adding a 2-hour bonus for wage work past 6 hours, and increasing this amount to a 100% bonus (what a US worker might call “double time”) for any wage work past 8 hours. As a broad rule, Atrusian employers who use wage labor would rather have two separate shift-teams to cover a full day of labor than dip into overtime pay calculations.

Most Atrusian workers only travel about one neighborhood or so from their home to get to work, with services like public schools often sharing service to several adjacent neighborhoods. Local markets cover the majority of everyday purchases like grocery or street food, everyday clothing, and personal entertainment or recreational goods, while longer-term purchases may be done at the nearest suitable guildhall market.

### Standard Income

A wage-laborer working 5 days per short-week (the seven day week of Atrusius) earns between 36 and 60 daimos per day, if they never work overtime. Over 5 days, this brings them to 180-300 daimos per week (the standard pay rate is short-weekly, at the end of the final shift), which is 1,36 - 2,12 gìldèn. Over the span of a month, this totals out to 7g 72d - 12g 72d, with 3 additional weeks’ of pay over the course of a year (about every 4 months).

Standard wage laborers are typically paid every 5th day of work, or once per short-week at the end of their last shift. This is a three day window encompassing the Thursdays, Fridays, and Saturdays of each week.

Due to the lack of an employment agreement or contract, Free Labor wages are paid out at the end of every single work shift. For simplicity, most Atrusian employers pay full-hour rates.

Higher status, and often higher-paying, jobs may have less frequent pay cycles such as long-weekly, twice-monthly, or even monthly, with the frequency of pay decreasing with the expected size of the payment. Foremen and guild service employees, civil servants, and many other bureaucratic jobs pay fortnightly, generally paid on the Kuhajia of each long week. 

A minimum standard 6-hour day over a 5/7 short week will earn a wage laborer about 1g 72s, or about USD 540, per short week, and over a 6-ish week month they will earn about 7g 72s, or about USD 2,700 per month.

### Raises

In most professions, raises are offered and assessed regularly, typically at a notable, but low, pergrossage of the base pay for the position. There is usually assessment for raises at least twice per year, and raises of less than _tomo_ (twelve) tendi (12t) are especially rare, with most employees earning at least _lammo_ (twenty-four) tendi (24t) in raises over a year.

### Standard Housing

> [!info] Atrusian Terminology
> 
> Where an Atrusian housing option is described with a number of rooms, this is equivalent to USA Gaeans decribing the number of bedrooms in a house or home. For example, a 1-room house actually usually has three or four actual rooms: a Common Room, a Kitchen, one Personal Room (equivalent to a bedroom), and possibly a toilet (some public housing may have shared toilet rooms or bathing rooms, and personal toilet rooms may not always include facilities for private bathing).

#### Public Housing

**Public housing** is a common option on Atrusius, with cities owning housing that is maintained by investing tax revenue. Public housing is usually the cheapest option for decent housing, but has the caveat of being a limited resource that may not always be available -- and is often reserved for the sake of citizens rather than travelers. On average, a month of public housing costs about 1g for a single-room home up to 1g 72d for a four-room home (which can usually support a family of up to 6 comfortably). Public housing is often packed tightly in urban areas, but still almost always includes a communal greenyard for the “block.”

A one-room public housing flat is around 480-600 square rods, while a four-room public housing flat is around 1440-1728 square rods. At the lowest cost, standard public housing costs roughly 3d 39t per day. Public housing costs may be paid at a Revenuer’s Office at any time, including partial payments at no charge, but must paid by the second Friday of each month to avoid the risk of eviction.

#### Personal Housing

**Personal housing** is also common, and usually includes space between houses, even if only alleyways from the outer streets to the greenyard. Greenyards are still typically communal, though many personal dwelling blocks allow a small area for personal gardening within these greenyards. On average, a month of taxes, maintenance and necessary services for a personal house ranges from about 1g 72d to 2g 72d for sizes similar to those in the public housing definition.

Personal housing costs an average of 50-75g by lot area, often paid with a loan from a banking guild which allows long-weekly payments over periods of up to 5-10 years. Construction (and demolition) of the house on a property are permitted but bring their own expenses separate from those of simply purchasing and maintaining a lot property.

The greatest benefit of personal housing is the ability to design and construct one’s own house, which allows many professionals to make a workplace on their ground floor where they can produce their own products or provide their own services, such as healers hosting a clinic, an artist’s studio, or even individual-level artisan production of goods like furniture, clothing, and even small local restaurants. The convenience of combining housing and business makes personal housing highly desirable for professional adults (and couples) in their 20s or older. Professional quality standards still apply to such business spaces when a profession calls for such standards, such as food service or clinical healing.

#### Private Housing

**Private housing** exists in two primary forms: _rejodomya_ and _damédomya._

##### Rejodomya

A **_rejodomya_** (sometimes _rejodom_) is a family estate house, usually found near the edges, or entirely outside, of cities and walled or fenced off. They are entire land holdings, and may or may not include side houses for servants. Families who live within a rejodomya are often considered to be nobility, even if they do not actually have a formal title, and servants who live there are often associated to the family as well. Rejodomya may be built and laid out according to the wishes of the family, and usually have a broad greenyard between the wall and the house, providing a sense of both isolation and open space. This is the private household property option for _residents_ of a city who are well enough off to support servants, whether temporary waged servants or live-in servants.

##### Damédomya

**_Damédomya_** is the name for a rental property owned by citizens rather than the public, but licensed by the city to be rented out to non-citizens. They are built on lots purchased as personal lots (sometimes in specified neighborhoods), and in general, they may built on similar principles to either public or personal housing. However, instead of paying rent or taxes directly to the city or upkeep costs to craftsfolk, the resident of a damédom pays a rent to the private owner (_jaljan_, lit. “landlord”) who is responsible for the taxes and upkeep costs. These types of houses are commonly used by travelers who will be in a city for significant periods of time, but who are not becoming long-term residents. This is comparable to a _pied-à-terre_ or condominium (particularly a fully owned condo, though some landlords may also use timeshare-like arrangements) there are specific fees involved in converting personal housing into _damedomu_ in most cities.

#### Commercial Housing

**Commercial housing** also exists, primarily for travelers who do not need or want long-term housing. This ranges from flop houses and communal rooms to dedicated short-term lodging houses akin to motels or hotels. This form of housing is not generally factored into the economy, and prices are based on a combination of the locale, the room area, and quality of the rooming being offered. In general, a short-term commercial housing option will cost more than a _damedom_ over the course of a month, but allows the flexibility of only paying for the number of days and nights a traveler requires.

### Standard Cost of Living

In general, the expected cost of living for most citizens is about the same as the cost of personal housing in the area. This means that as a broad average, most Atrusians in cities will probably need about 4-5 gìldèn per month to cover their housing and all other necessary personal expenses. This leaves 2g 72d for all other expenses (comforts, treats, luxuries, and improvements) for low-income wage laborers.

If we take the minimum standard income for a “full-time” wage laborer on Atrusius and apply the costs of living and housing, we get:

- **Public Housing, 1-room:** 7g 72s income (USD 2,700), less 1g (USD 360) for housing, less 2g 72s (USD 900) for cost of living, leaves 4g (USD 1,440) of personal money for the month.
- **Public Housing, 4-room:** 7g 72s income (USD 2,700), less 1g 72s (USD 540) for housing, less 2g 72s (USD 900) for cost of living, leaves 3g 72s (USD 1,260) of personal money for the month.
- **Personal/Rental Housing, 1-room:** 7g 72s income (USD 2,700), less 1g 72s (USD 540) for housing, less 2g 72s (USD 900) for cost of living, leaves 3g 72s (USD 1,260) of personal money for the month.
- **Personal/Rental Housing, 4-room:** 7g 72s income (USD 2,700), less 2g 72s (USD 900) for housing, less 2g 72s (USD 900) for cost of living, leaves 2g 72s (USD 900) of personal money for the month.

## Guilds

Outside of wage labor, many professions and trades are regulated by guilds, which assess the quality of each professional or tradesperson’s work regularly, to regulate the amount and degree of competition within a local market, and to foster international exchanges of knowledge and labor within their respective professions or trades. In addition to this, guilds serve as intermediaries for inter-professional trade, ensuring that their members can all receive comparable materials at comparable prices without needing to negotiate individual contracts with traders, merchants, or customers.

### Guild Market

Most guildhalls for productive trades will offer two separate markets: One for their members and one for the public. The member market is primarily for materials and tools used in the trade, while the public market sells the products of the trade. This is the equivalent of typical fantasy specialty shops such as potion, weapon, armor, and similar stores or markets. It is actually rare to purchase things directly from their manufacturer -- business conducted in workshops or cottages is usually either direct commission or maintenance and repairs.

### Merchants’ Guilds

Merchants’ Guilds are generally not marketplaces open to the public, but are coalitions of international shipping and caravan traders, with their halls often serving as warehouses for goods that are waiting to be transported either to local merchants or to other nations. Local merchants are generally not regulated in the way international traders or skilled crafts and professions are, but most such local merchants also do their own work or operate as a family business without a formal wage structure.

### Culinary Guilds

Related to merchants’ guilds, culinary guilds focus specifically on the trade and trading of foodstuffs, cookware, food storage, and seasonings. While restaurants are not regulated by the culinary guilds, they often do business with them to secure supplies of ingredients and equipment.

### Adventurers’ Guilds

Adventurers’ Guilds are found in every walled settlement, and often even have resthouses in or near smaller settlements without walls. Adventurers’ Guilds are a central element of the economic and civic structures of Atrusius, with most Guildhalls holding broad licenses allowing their members to engage in local delves within the secured holdings of the city or nation where they operate, and operating as banks for their members and economic pressure valves to prevent massive inflation. They often employ a moderate civilian workforce for management of recruits, liaisons between the civic and adventuring sides of life, and the management (or at least assistance in such) of many civil aspects of adventurer life. Each Guildhall also has a board of Master Adventurers who evaluate work contracts from the public (or state!) for difficulty and pricing.

Adventurers’ Guilds will often subsidize the healing, including mental health care, and maintenance costs of their members, charging these expenses from the dues charged after each quest while still ensuring their members are financially solvent. They will also set up retirement stipend accounts for members to survive a minimum of two years after retirement, with the payout of these stipends depending on the length, wealth, and accolades the individual brings in throughout their career.

Among the civic functions, Adventurers’ Guilds often operate shared living spaces for adventurers, which may range from flophouses which are little more than a bed and a storage space to co-habiting arrangements in 1-4 room houses, to sometimes entire city blocks in an Adventurers’ District with greenyards where Adventurers are able to socialize.

The most broadly spread Adventurers’ Guild, sometimes simply called “The Adventurers’ Guild,” is officially named “The Guild of the Many-Talented” and is symbolized with a bright forest-green to teal-green leaf that resembles a Gaean Maple Leaf. Technically, the Guild of the Many Talented is a coalition which combines the efforts of smaller profession-oriented guilds, the equivalent of “Class” guilds in traditional fantasy, which focus on the specific skills various types of adventurers specialize in.

## Banking

Banking is most often done through guilds, whether through one’s actual professional guild or a dedicated banking guild. Accounts with banks are generally categorized as Holding, Payment, or Investment accounts, and most adults have at least a joint Holding and Payment account -- the equivalent of a checking account, with the option of scheduled payments (like housing) for recurring expenses.

### Holding Accounts

Holding accounts are full reserve banking accounts, requiring the bank to secure and hold every tendi of an account available for full withdrawal. This is the most common type of account, and while it does not bear interest, it is highly secure even for low-income workers. These are equivalent to a modern Savings account except that because they operate on full reserve, the money cannot be used by the bank to generate interest.

### Payment Accounts

Payment accounts are accounts which allow the client to make payments without explicitly withdrawaing the money beforehand by writing a personal cheque. These cheques are provided by the bank—usually at no cost—in bundles of 48-72 with sequential issue numbers and the client’s public account information, tying the cheque to a specific client and account. Cheques may be used with any merchant, but are usually accepted only if the cheque can actually be cashed out: Cheques are primarily only useful within cities or towns which have an actual guildhouse for the specific banking guild.

Many payment accounts also allow the client to set up scheduled payment debits for recurring payments like news-sheet or postal subscriptions or the local Revenuer’s office (or the banking guild) for housing payments. During visits, a banking clerk can also inform clients if their current balance and upcoming scheduled payments are well aligned—while this is considered a courtesy, it is a regular practice.

Payment accounts are often conjoined with a Holding account, providing stability and flexibility even for low-income wage laborers. Most Atrusians have a banking account, and are not always carrying around, or hoarding in their homes, all of their wealth.

### Investment Accounts

Investment accounts are a partial reserve account, allowing the bank to manage investments and loans with the client’s money while guaranteeing a certain percentage to be available for withdrawal and regular maturation of interest. Such accounts typically offer a minimum average of 2t 12x interest on the daimos per year (roughly 1.5% APY), with quarterly maturation deposits (about every 132 days) based on the balance of the account on the first day of January, April, July, and October.

Because investment accounts do not allow their full balance to be withdrawn at once, they provide a degree of stability. The client may decide if the maturation pays into a separate account, including a portional split and which other account such maturations pay out to.
