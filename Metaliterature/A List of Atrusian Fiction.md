#### **_kuLarijmo Shan_**

> [!info] Information  
> - **Author:** (Adin)
> - **Conceit:** Epic History
> - **Genres:** Adventure, Action, Morality

A two-year fictional history in which the displaced ruler of a kingdom is pushed to claim a sacred sword, renew his family’s covenant with a divine being, and use the divine blessing to defeat the Emperor of the conquering nation. The world of _The Kingdom’s Crest_ has developed in a number of works produced by various authors over the years, with strongly gapped continuity, often involving centuries between individual stories.

#### **_Jènjal i Acenu_**

> [!info] Information  
> - **Authors:** Pol Almast (Adin)
> - **Conceit:** Slice of Life
> - **Genres:** Pastoral, Village Life, Growing Up

_Village of Animals_ is a slice of life story focused on growing up and exploring the world, told through the lens of fantasy anthropomorphic animals. The author is a professional anthropologist with a deep love for animals, and uses the story to bridge traditional and contemporary life paths for younger audiences before they are forced to confront the realities of adult life (whether with their own families or independently). The original series is pragmatic, trying to maintain a balance of the author’s ideal of balance, but it has also received several spin-offs with his blessing while the original is still under copyright until the start of the next year.

#### **_Jènjal i Acenu: Cici Jenesis_**

> [!info] Information  
> - **Authors:** kuRishax Agro (Anonymous)
> - **Conceit:** Slice of Life
> - **Genres:** Pastoral, Farming, Community, Romance

_The Sweet Life_ explores a utopian pastoral life in the setting of _Village of Animals._ It was created during the still-active copyright period of _Village of Animals_, by an old-fashioned young author who grew up in a rural community. The story of _The Sweet Life_ focuses on the life of a farmer (Rishax, literally meaning “Farm Worker”) who grows sugarreeds, leng’eni isa, and other sweets. It explored Rishax’s inheritance of the business from their parents and rebuilding their own relationships with the village residents after having left for an urban school. The climax of the story was Rishax’s marriage to the local Builder and Repairwoman, Lixai (literally meaning “Builder Girl”.) The author never used gendered pronouns for Rishax, they are only ever referred to using forms of the Adinjo _shi_, “they, them (singular).”

#### **_Keldin i Romu ~ ERYOL_**

> [!info] Information  
> - **Author:** Lekwan Orand (Kenyu)
> - **Conceit:** Keldin
> - **Genres:** Slice of Life, Community, Morality

A young kenyu man named Eryol discovers he has the power to shape the air around him, and decides to become a hero. His youthful energy and haste often cause follow-up troubles, and he must balance his academic, social, and heroic lives. The story keeps Eryol as a local hero, more interested in improving the lives of his city than in saving the world, and while his powers do grow in strength, they develop more in breadth throughout the story as he finds new ways to use his wind powers to serve the city.

#### _Rashi’hemlé!!_

> [!info] Information  
> - **Authors:** Team STAR
> 	- Lān Serā Merolar (Gramurn)
> 	- Talia Lanta (Kenyu)
> 	- Achsmul Herlits (Hol’tiin)
> 	- Réliin Myoxe (Elressie)
> - **Author:** Miklos Laumen (Adin)
> - **Conceit:** Speculative
> - **Genres:** Adventure, Comedy, Drama, Community

This series was simultaneously developed as a serialized novel and meten by a 4-member circle. It tells a fabulous story about a group of youth (adolescents, equivalent to about 15 year olds on Earth) who are recruited by aliens to help them convince interstellar society that Atrusius is a civilized planet. Each of the 4 years told the story from a different character’s perspective, reflecting the circle’s rotating peer-based leadership. Team STAR was made up of two male and two female authors, with Kenyu and Gramurn females, and Hol’tiin and Elressie males.

#### _Twanu i Rashi Paci_

> [!info] Information  
> - **Author:** Unattributed
> - **Conceit:** History
> - **Genres:** Epic, Adventure, Action, Morality

_The Black Star Books_ are a series that claims to be historical about the events of the Ancient War against the Tsel’vark. Each book is written from the perspective of one species—featuring their own focal character to reflect this—excluding the sixth book which is from the perspective of the five established heroes. It concludes with the destruction of a central Tsel’vark Hive and the flight of the Tsel’vark into the vast heavens, never to return.

The primary characters for each species are Pippo, the Peratan dilettante who has discovered a ring that allows him to sense the plans of the Zelvark; Tedzuren, the Semeric knight who has been granted a divine hammer by the God of the Deep Waters and who can leap ten rods high and thirty rods long; Admar the Hol’tiin Shaman who has been called to this quest by the elemental spirits hew worships; Lewin the Kenyu ranger appointed to guide the band into the Zelvark lands; and Xarm, the Gramurn agent, who focuses on keeping the party hidden and well supplied.

#### _POLLAN Domyatanu_ (The Pollan Household Stories)

> [!info] Information  
> - **Author:** Romance Circle
> 	- Jerin Romaren (Adin; Director _Young Lord Pollan and the Maid_, _The Maid and the Stablehand_)
> - **Conceit:** Epic
> - **Genres:** Adventure, Action, Morality

This story was split into three parts and written by a four member romantic specialty circle, and published in two separate magazines with the same publisher: One focused on young men and masculine tastes, the other focused on young women and feminine tastes, with the final book being published in both. One notable member of this circle is Jerin Romaren, who directed _Young Lord Pollan_, went on to become a well renowned author and illustrator of romantic comedies, and eventually worked with the Fourth Author to produce _I Love Bal Keldin!!_ which has just (373-Aug-16) been confirmed as receiving a third contract year.

##### Jandul POLLAN hi Dantaiti | Young Lord Pollan and the Maid

The Young Lord Pollan is recently of age and has begun to tease his sister’s grown maidservant, a Kenyu woman named Ellorië. Ellorië is initially very reluctant to pursue his teasing and flirtation, suspecting him of merely toying with her in a similar way to how he once teased and bullied his twin sister. When she feels she can no longer resist his pursuit without trouble, she chooses her words cautiously: “As you wish, my Lord.” As the end of the story, the Young Lord seems to be willing to renounce his title for Ellorië, which causes Ellorië to panic and flee from his chambers.

##### Jaitidul POLLAN hi Ashatanen | Young Lady Pollan and the Stablehand

The Young Lady Pollan is recently of age and has begun to take notice of the men working on her family estate. She has taken a particular interest in Ashauren, the stablehand who cares for her and her brother’s horses. Ashauren seems to be entirely smitten with the infatuated Lady, and begins to respond to her every wish with the light words, “As you wish, my Lady.” At the end of the story, the Young Lady is pushing Ashauren to go off on a grand quest to show her father his dedication to her, and after kissing him on the cheek, she returns to the manor house.

##### Dantaiti hi Ashatanen | The Maid and the Stablehand

Ellorië and Ashauren realize that their noble courting is not truly viable—the power of their employers is forcing both of them to comply with demands they are not ready for, and the elder Lord and Lady will no doubt be seeking honest courtships for the twins. This story is a sequel to both of the parallel stories of the Young Lord and Young Lady, and the titular Maid and Stablehand develop a genuine affection for each other while they are both expected to keep up the appearances for their noble family, remaining dutifully obedient even entertaining the appearance of being courted by the Young Lord and Young Lady.

At the end of the story, two new nobles are introduced, the Young Lady Romarel and the Young Lord Saychin, as the arranged partner for the Pollan twins. This reveal allows Ellorië and Ashauren to pursue their own courtship more freely, as the Pollan twins cannot openly use them for courtship “practice” any longer, but they are still bound to the Pollan family as servants.

#### The King of Gaurin: Gogon

A Gramurn story focused on a captured _gaurin_ dubbed Gogon by a wealthy noble (originally unnamed, but later identified as the “Lord of Farnell”) who has created an observational park for wild animals and monsters. The story begins with the noble setting off on a safari to find a gaurin in the southern jungles of Mermag, being warned off by the locals who say the king of the gaurin will seek vengeance if any gaurin are taken from their holy land.

Despite these warnings, the noble captures a giant gaurin (usually described as about 15-20 rods tall, though even in this story his size is not entirely consistent) and takes him across the seas to Kamelon. 

##### Legal Controversy

Parlaio Ellis attempted to sue Xelaun Erra Fashar for this story, claiming that the noble Lord of Farnell was intended to be a criticism of himself. Fashar argued that being in a fictional version of Atrusius, there was no reason to expect that the Kenyu nobleman and Lord of Farnell within his fictional story was meant to represent any actual Lord of Farnell, and that having never met the honorable Lord Ellis, he had no reason to attack his name or reputation: Farnell was simply a state that would justify transporting Gogon through Kamelon, which was the actual scene of the most noteworthy confrontations in the story. The courts carefully considered the arguments, and their final ruling was fierce:

Fashar was cautioned to be more cautious in the use of actual titles of nobility in future stories, and the precedent was set that the anonymous Noble was not a defense provided that the noble title in question was real.

However, Ellis was also cautioned that because the Lord of Farnell within the story was unnamed, his own actions drew greater scrutiny to possible parallels between himself and the fictional character (a phenomenon Gaean readers may recognize by the name “the Streisand effect”): There was no evidence within the text that the Lord of Farnell within the story was the current Lord of Farnell, and merely being a Kenyu was insufficient cause to assume a fictional Lord of Farnell was a commentary on the real Lord of Farnell.

In short, the court ruled that the fictional Lord of Farnell was not a threat to Lord Ellis and his reputation, but advised Fashar (and through the ruling, future authors) to increase the level of obvious fictionalization when depicting fictional characters with real titles.

Shortly after the ruling, the final chapter of _The King of Gaurin: Gogon_ was published. Ironically, the fame of this case made his name more popular, so he was able to write more stories and develop quite a fandom. Farshan occasionally wrote more stories about Gogon, often interacting with other monstrous creatures, and bringing sympathy to certain giant-sized beasts. Since 246, many other authors have also written Gogon stories, including meten, magishows, and magigames.

#### Lord of the Jungle: Shaxon

Lord Parlaio Ellis wrote this story as a standalone novel after his lawsuit against Fashar—some historical commentators suggest the lawsuit was a tool to publicize his name prior to publishing this story. The protagonist of the story is a Kenyu named Shaxon who was raised by a pride of apes on a fictional island in the Xato Archipelago, and is learning how to speak and behave in a proper Kenyu manner after meeting a greentender woman named Gailanna who is researching the local wildlife. While her focus is on the jungle cats, she takes a special interest in Shaxon, and in educating him.

In this debut story, Shaxon struggles to understand the Kenyu customs, but gradually develops enough mastery of Gailanna’s tongue to explain that he has lived in this land for as long as he remembers, and that his pride of apes is the only family he knows. In the middle of the story, an old greentender cabin is discovered, and Gailanna discovers that Shaxon was an infant when the cabin was last used—three partial Kenyu skeletons inside the cabin suggest that there was a great attack on the previous greentenders, and the damages suggest a large cat and a large ape fighting within the cabin. The elder of Shaxon’s tribe explains (through Shaxon) that his mate had heard the sound of an infant and fought off the jungle tiger to protect the baby ape — the same baby who had grown up to be Shaxon.

At the end of the story, Shaxon fights the old and bitter jungle tiger Lourram and avenges his birth parents’ deaths — but no longer feels entirely at home among his pride, and sets off on a journey to Aureb with Gailanna.

Ellis wrote five additional Shaxon books, and his first meten (published in 268) was an adaptation of the series. He stopped producing traditional literature after adapting this story to a meten, and helped fund the Mighty Meten publishing house to ensure he could always keep publishing his stories.

#### Belorion: The Nobleman from the Dark World

After discovering a wild portal to a kingdom that appears on no maps of Atrusius, the Kenyu lesser noble, Yochan Kennyon, finds himself invited to the home of the great and noble Lord Belorion, a man who appears at a glance like any other Kenyu, but for being exceedingly pallid and having eyes the color of blood. Kennyon tells of the portal and of how nobody seems to know his nation of Angar here — Lord Belorion assures him that he has long sought to visit Angar, but the portal has eluded him. He suggests that if Kennyon finds the portal to return home, he may be able to leave a beacon and invite the Lord across the portal.

Through the course of the story, Kennyon does just that, helping the Lord Belorion establish a foothold in Angar City and connecting him with a legal counsel familiar with the local customs and laws. It is gradually revealed that Belorion is a vampire and is feeding his need for blood on innocents, but by this time, Kennyon can do little but write and publish his protests: he has fallen under the mental dominion of Belorion and cannot refuse to obey him.

Eventually, an Adin knight by the name of Kitasha Lorebach arrives in Angar and is able to identify Lord Belorion as a vampire without falling subject to his dominion. She confronts Kennyon who has been the Lord’s legal agent, around the time that Kennyon’s wife (Nimuël) is taken into the thrall of the Lord. Ultimately, Kennyon works with Lorebach and her partner, Baerelon, to wrest his wife from the manipulations of Lord Belorio. 

This story is the classical archetype of modern vampire fiction on Atrusius, establishing many of the conventions of both vampires and vampire fiction. Most notably, Belorion established the idea that Vampire powers (though not Vampires themselves) are weakened in sunlight, are related to the element of wind, and that they have effortless charm, but also that they must be invited into personal dwellings, that they dislike (or are weakened by) garlic, and that they dislike other spiritual, magical, or dark world entities.

The author of Belorion published the story as Yochan Kennyon, and due to the loss of certain records, nobody is entirely certain what the author’s real name was.

> [!info] Vampires & The Dark World
> 
> The Dark World is a real phenomenon on Atrusius, and portals to this realm can be found in nature, particularly in ungoverned wilderness regions or dungeons. The manafield is about four times more dense in the Dark World, causing the entire alternate dimension to be composed primarily of dungeons, with the sapient species there being much heartier than the normal Atrusian species. Because of the density of the manafield, he sky of the Dark World is a deeper rosy red color than the pinkish color of Atrusius, and while the native wildlife is similar, the sapient species on the other side of the portal are distinct from those in the primary world of Atrusius.
> 
> Vampires are one such sapient species, and the most socially and militarily dominant. While they are largely hairless like the primate-related species of Atrusius, they are more closely related to the Atrusian varieties of bats. Fictional depictions often have powers that are not really associated with them: most notably, the mesmeric mind control abilities associated with them in fiction are not true, and their inherent physical abilities are not affected by the cycle of day and night. Vampires are sensitive to certain herbal compounds, the most potent commonplace poisonous plant to them being the Atrusian allium — onions and garlic.
> 
> Despite the different species origins, the degree of mana suffusing Vampires actually grants them a high fertility rate with other species, allowing them to reproduce sexually with other species if they desire to engage in such activities, and the microbiome that enables them to metabolize mana from blood can be passed on to other species through the consumption of their blood, even in fairly small amounts.

#### The Dew of the Mountains

An agricultural family rivalry between the Narok and Leÿpegn families set in Omasheden, an Orkish settlement in northern Hyarem, escalates into a deep feud over their farming borders, garbling defenses, and a forbidden series of trysts between the elder children of both families. While the primary story explores the forbidden friendship, and eventual trysts, between Kerra Narok and Moragn Leÿpegn, there are regular and major interludes exploring seven generations of family rivalry and feuding that have led to the current state.

> [!info] Generational Storytelling
> 
> The tradition of generational storytelling is actually a highly significant element of Orkish literary structures, fictional and non-fictional. This is often attributed to their short life span, but this genre of story construction gives them a particular insight into the passage of time that many of the longer-lived species still struggle to accept that Orks comprehend—by understanding the perspectives of generations leading up to the present, the Orks both retain their urgent lifestyle pacing and mentality, while honoring the many similarly short generations that have led up to the present.
> 
> A few Orkish authors even argue that they may have a better sense of what the passage of time truly means and why it is important that young leaders be allowed to rise quickly with thorough education, while the longer-lived species often argue that leadership requires long developed knowledge and experience.

#### Three Moons: Three Realms

This is an Angarul Kenyu story that predates the Atrusian printing press. While the original author is unknown, it is considered the foundational story of the otherworld genre on Atrusius, establishing the earliest form of portal fantasy tropes: Naturally occurring portals between Atrusius, the Dark World, and the Greenwild, and presence of a helpful figure to the Atrusian traveler within the otherworld.

The story is written from a first-person perspective as a Kenyu who has supposedly traveled to the courts of the Elements and Spirits, as well as to the fortresses and strongholds of the Vampires, Gnolls, and other cultures of the Dark World. It is notable in that it reads like a hybrid of modern travelogue journals and cultural observations journals, with very little of the exaggeration found in later otherworld stories like _BELORION._

The story posits that the portals to each world are strongest and easiest to find under the new and full phases of their respective moons, with a full Gerjui making it easiest to find portals back to Atrusius from the other worlds, while a full Gurion makes it easier to find portals to the Dark World and a full Hyfel makes it easier to find portals to the Greenwild. The theory is difficult to confirm or deny, as such portals are unpredictable—even if one does know where to find them, they do not appear to form on any consistent schedule.

#### The Great Hunts of Ferendÿlb

One of the great legends of Orkish culture, this myth teaches the moral value of confidence and aspiration—and the line between confidence and hubris.

It is an epic cycle composed of roughly 40 smaller epic poems, but the most well-known is the story of Ferendÿlb’s last and greatest hunt: Having killed every beast of the land (and underworld), Ferendÿlb vows to slay one of every beast in the seas, and also in the skies. In this final chapter, he eventually succeeds in his quest of killing a sky dragon, and then realizing he has one beast of the sky yet to slay—a rk’nary like the one he is riding—he allows the rush of victory to conquer his judgement, and kills his own mount, falling to his own death with the dead bird.

#### The Journey to the West

A generational mythical tale of the early Serobalÿr Orks and their journey west from Falkena, then through the harsh steppes of Xardon and across the mountains into Hyarem, eventually founding their capital city—Ÿshkaren—in a rich, fertile woodland at the foot of a mountain range. This is not a modern character-driven account, but an etiology and legendary history of the Serobalÿr. It is generally treated as true even if it exaggerates details, though it is also the foundation for many modern character-driven stories of individuals who seek liberation from the violence of their past, especially for the itinerant and reluctant hero archetype.
