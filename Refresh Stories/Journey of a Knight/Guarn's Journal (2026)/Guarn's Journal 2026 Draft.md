# Guarn’s Journal (2026 Edition)

## 1. Mornings

I hate the morning. My siblings say I’m like a feralist, the way I prefer the night. But for Tomo and Jésen, I’ll try. I’ve checked the fire in our den, the meat from last night is getting very tender. The bones are coming out of the pot naked, too. I’ll just have a small bite before I get my things ready.

A spear. A bow. The hooded cloak I was given after my first kill on the hunt. I won’t be wearing it during the day unless I want to dry myself out, of course. It’s going to be a hot day, and any gramurn worth their spit would rather stay still and cool until the sun goes down…

So maybe I’m not worth my spit. Or maybe my friends are worth more than my spit? Tomo has been my friend since we started school. There’s almost nothing I wouldn’t do for him. Jésen has been my friend since that first day Tomo brought him over to play cross in the schoolyard. We were on opposite teams, but he was good. too good to believe he hadn’t played before.

But not good enough at talking to make normal friends. Not back then, at least.

I finished preparing a bowl of pulled meat and broth, and took a deep breath. It was only right to thank the animal for providing another meal, so I dropped to my haunched, bowed my head, and thought the prayer of the feast.

I looked to the skypaper windows and saw that it was very bright out. I slurped the broth and devoured the portion of meat and vegetables. Then I headed out for the market. I was going to need good arrows and a new net. After all, nanri had warned me against taking the pack’s equipment on this trip, but I had been working hard and saving my money. I’ve saved nearly a gilden in daimos coins, and it’s time to show my new pack how generous I am.

# Guarn’s Journal (2026 Edition)

## 2. Purchases

As I stepped out of the den, I let out a deep yawn. I don’t know why the morning light makes me feel so tired, but going outdoors always makes it worse. We live near the edge of Karishé, in a traditional den. We’re farmers, after all, responsible for some of the biggest fields west of the walls. But we’re Gramurn, too. We are hunters, and I’ve been training with the striders since my first kill.

I don’t want to be stuck on the farm. I want to travel the world. So of course I’ll help my friends with their journey to the east. I’ve learned the gamelands, the pasturelands, and the desert around us. I’ve studied the maps and the woodlands, though I’ve never been to the dense woodlands before.

As I walk to the market, I hear the voice before he sees me. Tomodak, my best friend! Can I get myself behind him before he realizes? Well, there’s only one way to know!

I slow my pace, stepping softly. His ears are more sensitive than they look, so I need to be very quiet to sneak up on him. But it’s nothing unfamiliar to a hunter. I breathe smoothly. Calmly.

“The last spool was half the price,” Tomodak grumbled out to the merchant. “Why is it so expensive now?”

“Well, there’s been a uptick in demand,” the merchant said with a sly feline smile. One of the Elressie traders. A pair of glass lenses rested before his eyes as he held up the spool. “Besides, you arrre quite a young man, good customer. Do your parrrents approve of you leaving the walls at night?”

“I am an adult,” Tomodak said flatly, though I could hear the undertone of anger.

I stepped closer while he was focused on the deal. I saw the eyes of the merchant shift to me and I lunged, wrapping my forepaws around Tomodak’s face, covering his eyes. “Who’s got your back?”

Tomodak growled at first, but as soon as he recognized my voice it shifted to laughter. “Guarn! My oldest friend!”

The merchant knocked his own knuckles on the stall. “Sirrrs, I see you are friendly, but please, we are haggling, arrre we not?”

“Haggling?” I said, releasing my friend’s eyes. “I don’t know that my friend is sociable enough to recognize that. Tell me, what’s the price you want?”

Tomodak’s cheeks grew dark as I spoke, but it was a tactical embarrassment. I would apologize later. The merchant needed my attention. “6 daimos,” the merchant answered.

“I see. That does seem high if the last spool my holy friend purchased was half as much.” I took a breath. “Do we really need another spool, friend?”

His eyes shifted to the side as he said, “Better to be prepared than needy.”

“I see, yes. Well, merchant-sami, I can respect your need for money. And your obviously low supply, but we two rookie adventurers simply can’t afford six daimos.”

“Then you can’t afford a sparrre,” the merchant snapped, looking us over.

“I can definitely pay three daimos,” Tomodak said. “I just don’t see why a roll of silver thread should cost six times its own weight.”

“I understand the work that goes into producing such a fine material,” I said. “But my friend is right. This is a survival good, not for woven inlays. 3 daimos.”

The merchant looked us over and narrowed his slitted eyes. “I suppose I do already have more on order… I could let this one go for 5 daimos.”

“Five, five?” I paused and held out my bare forepaw. “Unlike you, I cannot count this high so easily. Four daimos for my four fingers, and I will count you as a good friend.”

There was a moment of tension in the air. Thick like incense, or a block of lard. “Fine, my good frrriend. Four daimos, but you are stealing my lunch. As friends you understand the debt of such a deal.”

“Naturally,” I said, taking a deep breath. I reached into my purse and pulled out two coins. Glancing over them quickly, I slid them into my open palm, offering them to the merchant. “With a three and a one, our business is settled, yes?”

I love my dear friend, but for all the confidence he has, he struggles with the niceties of market transactions. The merchant took the coins and handed me the spool. I passed the purchase on to Tomo, and then we proceeded on our way.

# Guarn’s Journal (2026 Edition)

## 3. Making Camp

The day was eventful. Jésen was spiraling into obsessive control. Tomodak was trying to replace Jésen’s fears. As we ate the wrapped pies for lunch, I took stock and became the mediator of the two as usual. “It is true we must be prepared for days we cannot, or fail to, hunt, Tomo. But these ration packs will accumulate, and we are carrying all of our good on ourselves, Jésen. Perhaps we can simply choose a smaller number of rations, and you can trust in us to keep the water and fresh food.”

Tomodak agreed readily, but it took some time, and my skills of negotiation, to get Jason down to a more reasonable 7 days of rations each. We washed our hands of lunch, and prepared the kit for our dry run. Soon we were off on the road.

Daylight travel is so different from nighttime. It’s harder to see the movements of prey, for one thing. Oh, grasses rustle, but the warmth is missing, and that is why we hunt at night most of the time.

I stopped several times to point out plants useful for survival. The mallepip can be found in clusters near places where prey animals defecate. It shows the soil is recovering, but more importantly, it shows that there is moisture nearby if it is healthy. It is a thirsty plant, and has shallow roots, so it will wither in only a few days if there is no water nearby.

The leyroot only grows along the top of elemental leystreams, and if it is blue, you can draw water from the petals with a light magical pressure. It won’t generate enough to last a day, but if you follow the water leystream you are bound to find a spring or stream.

We reach a good camping site after several hours. It’s an elevated part of the pasturelands, with a good view out over the land. I’ll dig the firepit, and the other necessary pits for our expedition. Once Tomo gets back with the wood, I’ll start the fire and prepare our dinner.

I’ll also take the watch until the end of the dark hour. If we survive that, we should be fine for the rest of the night.

Guarn Waila Errukxan, Wildstrider  
358, Nernemesen, 40  
Karishé Pasturelands
