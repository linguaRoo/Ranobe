# The Brief

## I

Toyland was abandoned in the 1980s. It’s been more than 30 years since any worker or inspector has entered it, and nobody knows why the Toy Company abandoned this factory. The company is one of the big ones, still active today, but the factory just… closed down.

Here’s what we know:

The factory was originally built in 1927, but was temporarily shuttered during the Depression. They re-opened to support the war effort, and by 1949 they were back to producing toys and select school supplies. The factory was bought out by the big company in the 60s, and it was supposedly used to make test lines. The first Charlie the Cheetah models were supposedly developed and assembled here, before they hit the primary market.

In the 1970s there were a series of incidents involving test toy lines. The Li’l Brudder dolls were an unmitigated disaster and inspired those killer doll movies. Research suggests they were using toxic paints that made some of the kids who got test models suffocate while snuggling up with their Li’l Brudders in bed, and the ones who didn’t all became very violent.

Following that, the Friendly Monster line was introduced ripping off Muppet designs. Freddy Frog, Charlotte Cat, Derek Dog, and Lucy Llama. Almost every kid who got one said that the eyes were following them everywhere. A few caused incidents after taking their Friendly Monsters to playdates. They said their monster told them to do the things they did… and that they had to do them to be good friends.

In 1979, they released a baby doll, Little Lisa Angel, and… well, the stories about her are anything but angelic. There’s a common rumour that they were repurposing the materials and processes from Li’l Brudder, but the stories? Come on, you _must_ have heard the stories about the Little Lisa Angels.

In the 80s they cut back down to plush dolls and action figures. The plush dolls seemed fine, based on cute monsters, they almost started to clear the public image. And then the Fix-It figures. Emergency services vehicles that turned into a humanoid robot of their corresponding profession. Yeah, they were a knock-off of that show, but more like the Pup Patrol is today.

Unfortunately, it seems like the Fix-Its were tainted by something from the old Li’l Brudders, and… the fire trucks started fired, the police cars intimidated their kids, the ambulances… well, I don’t like repeating all the headlines. In ‘88, they closed the factory down, but kept security on until the mid-90s. But now, it’s 2025.

Every few years, some teens sneak in there. Usually they come back out, scared, rambling. But alive. Once in a while, they don’t. Sometimes the police go in. Last year, they didn’t come back out…

That’s where I come in. Urban exploration. Along with my crew. We had scouted it. Dug up the old blueprints. We had a plan.

---

April 18. Friday night, 10 PM. We were ready. The main entrance was well known. Far corner from the main roads, concrete wall had an iron door set into it. The lock was busted. Police checked on it every few weeks, threw a chain up. The chain never really lasted long. One of our rules in urbex is that we don’t damage anything if we can help it, so we had to watch. Make sure the chains were down before out night.

The stocky lady by the wall signaled us. “Let’s head in,” I said. “One at a time, wait just inside the door. Don’t forget the plan.”

The darker-skinned man who left first carried a duffel bag. Ropes, first aid, water bottles, snacks. “I gotcha, boss,” he said. Monty. He was a newer member of the team, only joined us in the new year. He was a climber, and he was independent before. I think he’s about 28. “Three meters, right?”

I nodded. This wasn’t our first exploration, and we always kept a tight perimeter when we first entered. This was a bigger one than usual, too. The Toyland campus was huge, between the offices, the factory, and the warehouses, and some previous explorers had suggested that what we could see from the surface was barely half of the complex. I didn’t know whether to trust those rumours, but we were prepared for the possibility.

A signal from the flashlight. Monty was in. “Mana?”

The blonde girl with pigtails nodded. She was our scout. Rope. A collapsible rod. Lockpicks. She pulled up the indigo hood on her jumper, tucking her pigtails inside, and started to scurry across the way. She knew her stuff, we’d been breaking into locked classrooms at school since we were 12. And she had a knack for spotting hazards I often overlooked. We made a good team.

I waited for the signal to confirm she was in. Tifa was next. Black hair hung around her face, even with the plaited tail of hair behind her. She was muscle. Forcing doors, clearing debris. She was clever too, my big sister, but sometimes a bit too clever for her own good. “You ready, sis?”

“When am I not ready?” Her leather jacket was zipped up. She had a spare first aid kit, a few knives, and sturdy boots. I gave her a nod, and she sprinted down the path, heading for the entrance where Jules was standing guard.

I waited for the signal and then locked the van. I headed down the hill, wearing a cowboy hat and tight denim shorts, a comfortable green t-shirt, and a backpack with all of our documentation gear. Four cameras, two standard camcorders, various alleged paranormal activity detectors like EMF sensors and a thermal camera, and my trusty walking stick: 6 feet long, ash, with a bear’s head carved into the top, and a sturdy brass cap on the bottom. With a good swing, it was plenty of defense on the road -- and should be more than enough to deal with any pests that might be nesting in Toyland.

I got to the breach we were using to get in, and gave Jules a hug, the stocky lady my first girlfriend and the partner who really pushed me into this stuff. She was built like a brick wall, in a good way. Stubborn, tough as nails, and her green eyes always made me melt a bit. She was also muscle, like Tifa, but where Tifa was the kind of muscle that made things happen, Jules was that kind that kept them done.

Together, we went through the open gate, letting it close behind us.
