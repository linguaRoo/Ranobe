# Skypaper

Skypaper is a form of paper made from thin sheets of interwoven reeds, treated with animal glue (traditionally) or other waterproofing materials. Because the reeds split easily into very thin strips and sheets, it provides a durable, weatherproofed material that diffuses light, and is traditionally used for windows, movable dividers, and even interior walls and doors, similar to Japanese _shoji_.

It is also often used as a weatherproof wrapping for rations and packages, and can be painted before it is sealed to give it rich patterns and colors, while reducing the translucency with thick paints, or coloring the diffused light with thin paints. In this way it is also comparable to parchment paper, wax paper, and modern wrapping paper.

Additional sealing treatments can also be applied to make the paper firmer without reducing its translucency, though it is more common to layer sheets to make firmer architectural pieces _because_ layering them reduces the translucency.

Modern skypaper can be made from reed pulp, and is less rigid than traditional woven skypaper, but can also be made in an unsealed form as a cheap writing material, and unlike the woven form which can only be marked easily on one side, the pulp-based skypaper can be textured on both side for writing practice. It is the cheapest form of mass manufactured paper on Atrusius, and if sealed after being marked, can preserve writing for extended periods.

There are pests that will try to eat skypaper, especially unsealed skypaper, and some are able to consume or dissolve the sealing agents. This means that it is always important to monitor that status of skypaper over the long term, and it often needs to be replaced regularly.
